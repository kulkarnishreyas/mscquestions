Week 3 — Descriptive & Inferential Statistics

Sharpe Ratio Insight (dispersion application)

Risk-adjusted return: Sharpe = (Rp − Rf) / σp A higher Sharpe = more return per unit of standard deviation (risk).

Monte Carlo Simulation in Inferential Stats

Simulate 1,000 portfolio scenarios to assess the probability of achieving a target return — the dispersion of simulated outcomes informs the risk profile.

Pearson Correlation Coefficient (formula recap)

r = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) / √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² · Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²]

Range: −1 to +1. Captures linear association only.

Week 4 — Time Value of Money

Problem 1 — Present Value

Find the present value of ₹1,000 received in 5 years if the discount rate is 5%.

Solution

PV = 1000 / (1 + 0.05)⁵ ≈ ₹783.53

That is, ₹1,000 received in 5 years is equivalent to ₹783.53 today at a 5% discount rate.

Problem 2 — Future Value: Annual vs Quarterly Compounding

Compare ₹5,000 invested at 6% for 5 years under annual compounding.

Solution

FV = 5000 × (1 + 0.06)⁵ ≈ ₹6,691.13

Quarterly compounding gives a slightly higher amount due to more frequent compounding; monthly higher still.

Problem 3 — Inflation-Adjusted FV

₹10,000 grows at 7% annually for 10 years, with inflation of 3%. Find nominal and real FV.

Solution

FVnominal = 10000 × (1.07)¹⁰ ≈ ₹19,671.51
FVreal = 19671.51 / (1.03)¹⁰ ≈ ₹14,664.56

Inflation reduces actual purchasing power of the future value.

Problem 4 — FV of a Monthly Annuity

₹2,000 invested monthly at 8% annually for 10 years. Find the FV of the annuity.

Solution

FVannuity = 2000 × [(1 + 0.08/12)¹²⁰ − 1] / (0.08/12) ≈ ₹3,64,573.27

Small regular investments compound substantially over 10 years.

Problem 5 — Loan Amortisation & Early Repayment

A ₹500,000 loan at 10% per annum for 5 years. Impact of an extra ₹5,000 annual payment?

Solution

  • Simple Interest: SI = P × r × t (interest on principal only)
  • Compound Interest: CI = P(1 + r/n)nt (interest on principal + accumulated)
  • Example: ₹100,000 at 10% annual → after 1 year, annual compounding = ₹110,000; quarterly slightly higher; monthly even higher.
  • Extra ₹5,000/yr on a ₹500,000 loan → reduces total interest paid and shortens repayment term.

Week 6 — Predictive ML

Logistic Regression — Loan Default Probability

If a logistic model returns P(Y=1) = 0.85 for an applicant, what does it mean?

Solution

An 85% predicted probability of default. The applicant is high-risk; depending on the threshold (often 0.5), this would be classified as "default".

Odds Ratio Interpretation

An odds ratio of 3.5 for the variable "previous defaults" means customers with past defaults are 3.5× more likely to default again than those without — holding other variables constant.

P-Value & Statistical Significance

If a variable's p-value > 0.05, it may be excluded from the model — it is not statistically significant at the 5% level. If the 95% confidence interval for the coefficient includes zero, the same conclusion applies.

Week 8 — Value at Risk (VaR) & Risk Management

Problem 1 — Historical Simulation VaR

For a ₹17 crore portfolio, the 10th worst daily return (out of the last 100 trading days, sorted ascending) is −0.75%. What is the VaR at 90% confidence?

Solution

VaR = 0.75% × ₹17,00,00,000 = ₹12,75,000

With 90% confidence, the loss is not expected to exceed ₹12,75,000 in a single trading day under historical conditions.

Problem 2 — Parametric (Variance-Covariance) VaR

At 95% confidence (Z = 1.65), portfolio standard deviation σ = 2%, time horizon t = 1 day.

VaR = Zα × σ × √t

Solution

VaR = 1.65 × 0.02 × √1 = 0.033 = 3.30%

i.e., the portfolio is expected to lose no more than 3.30% in a single day with 95% confidence under the normality assumption.

Conditional VaR (CVaR / Expected Shortfall)

CVaR is the average loss in scenarios where VaR is exceeded — offers a deeper view of tail risk than VaR alone.

Week 9 — Portfolio Management & Performance Measurement

Portfolio Optimisation — Standard Workflow (MPT)

  1. Define investment objectives
  2. Compute daily returns of each asset
  3. Calculate expected returns and risk (std dev) per asset
  4. Compute covariance / correlation matrix to evaluate diversification benefit
  5. Compute portfolio variance using weights and covariances
  6. Run mean-variance optimisation to find efficient frontier
