1. Explain the importance of financial time series data visualization.

A financial time series is a set of observations (price, return, volatility, volume) recorded in chronological order. Visualization is the process of presenting this data graphically — through line charts, candlestick charts, scatter plots, histograms and correlograms — so that analysts can interpret it intuitively. Raw numerical tables hide structure; a well-chosen chart makes that structure visible at a glance, which is why visualization is the essential first step (exploratory data analysis) before any formal modelling.

Why it is important:

  • Trend and pattern detection: line and candlestick charts reveal long-term upward/downward trends, cycles and structural breaks (e.g. a market crash) that summary statistics such as the mean cannot capture.
  • Seasonality and volatility clustering: repeating intra-year patterns and bursts of high volatility (calm periods followed by turbulent ones) become immediately obvious, guiding the choice of model.
  • Outlier and anomaly spotting: spikes, crashes and data-entry errors that would distort a model are caught instantly and can be investigated or cleaned.
  • Understanding relationships: scatter plots and overlaid series show correlation and co-movement between two assets, which is vital for diversification and hedging decisions.
  • Model selection and diagnostics: tools like the ACF/PACF correlogram help decide between AR, MA, ARMA or ARIMA models and check whether residuals behave like white noise.
  • Communication and decision-making: dashboards convey risk and performance clearly to managers, clients and regulators who are not statisticians, supporting faster and better-informed investment decisions.
2. Explain implied volatility in financial markets.

Implied volatility (IV) is the value of volatility that, when fed into an option-pricing model such as Black–Scholes, makes the model's theoretical price exactly equal the option's observed market price. Instead of computing volatility from a history of returns, IV is reverse-engineered ("backed out") from the prices investors are actually paying for options. It therefore reflects the consensus view of how volatile the underlying asset will be in the future.

Key points:

  • Forward-looking: unlike historical (realised) volatility, which is calculated from past returns, IV represents the market's expectation of future volatility over the remaining life of the option.
  • Effect on premiums: higher IV means greater expected uncertainty, which raises option premiums; lower IV reduces them. Volatility is the only Black–Scholes input that is not directly observable, so IV is central to pricing.
  • Volatility smile/skew: plotting IV against strike price for a fixed maturity typically produces a smile or skew rather than a flat line, revealing that the market does not believe returns are perfectly normally distributed.
  • Term structure: plotting IV against maturity shows how expected volatility varies over different horizons.
  • Market sentiment: indices such as the VIX aggregate implied volatility and are widely used as a "fear gauge" of market stress.
3. Describe the autoregressive (AR) model with an example.

An autoregressive (AR) model is a linear time-series model in which the current value of a series is explained by a weighted combination of its own past (lagged) values plus a random error term. The word "auto-regressive" literally means the variable is regressed on itself. A general AR(p) model of order p is written as:

Xt = c + φ1Xt-1 + φ2Xt-2 + … + φpXt-p + εt

where c is a constant, the φ's are the AR coefficients, p is the order (number of lags used) and εt is a white-noise error term.

  • It is used for stationary series and captures persistence or momentum — the idea that recent history influences the present.
  • The coefficients are usually estimated by ordinary least squares or maximum likelihood, and the order p is chosen using the ACF/PACF plots or information criteria (AIC/BIC).
  • Stationarity condition: for an AR(1) model the process is stationary only if |φ1| < 1; a value of exactly 1 gives a non-stationary random walk (unit root).
  • Example — AR(1): Xt = 0.7 Xt-1 + εt. If today's return is 2%, the predictable part of tomorrow's return is 0.7 × 2% = 1.4%, so returns partially carry over from one period to the next before random noise is added.
4. Explain the purpose of exponential smoothing and its advantages in time series analysis.

Exponential smoothing is a forecasting technique that predicts the next value of a series as a weighted average of past observations, where the weights decline exponentially (geometrically) as observations get older. The most recent data therefore receive the greatest weight. The simplest form (simple exponential smoothing) is:

St = α Xt + (1 − α) St-1,   0 < α < 1

where St is the smoothed value (forecast) and α is the smoothing constant.

Purpose: to smooth out random short-term noise and produce reliable short-term forecasts that adapt to recent changes in the data, without having to store or weight the entire history equally.

Advantages:

  • Simplicity and speed: it is easy to understand, computationally cheap and needs only the previous forecast and the latest observation, so very little data must be stored.
  • Controllable responsiveness: a large α makes the forecast react quickly to recent observations, while a small α produces a smoother, more stable forecast — the analyst tunes this to the data.
  • Adaptability: the method automatically adjusts as new data arrive, making it well suited to live/operational forecasting.
  • Extensions for richer data: Holt's method adds a trend component and the Holt–Winters method adds both trend and seasonality, so the family can handle most practical patterns.
  • Best suited to data without strong trend or seasonality in its simplest form; widely used in demand, sales and price forecasting where recent information matters most.
5. Explain the concept of long memory in financial time series.

Long memory (also called long-range dependence or persistence) describes a time series in which observations remain significantly correlated with observations from the distant past. The defining feature is that the autocorrelation function (ACF) decays slowly — hyperbolically — as the lag increases, in contrast to short-memory ARMA processes whose ACF decays rapidly (geometrically) to zero.

Key points:

  • Persistent shocks: a shock today continues to influence the series over many future periods rather than dying out quickly, so dependence "lingers".
  • Modelling: long memory is captured using fractional differencing, where the differencing parameter d takes a non-integer value (typically 0 < d < 0.5). This gives the ARFIMA (Autoregressive Fractionally Integrated Moving Average) model, a generalisation of ARIMA in which d need not be a whole number.
  • Where it appears: long memory is commonly observed in financial volatility and in squared and absolute returns and trading volume, even though raw returns themselves show little memory.
  • Diagnosis: it is detected through a slowly decaying correlogram and by estimating the Hurst exponent (H > 0.5 indicates long memory).
  • Importance: ignoring long memory leads to under-estimation of long-horizon risk and poor volatility forecasts.
6. Differentiate between the GARCH, GARCH-M and Exponential GARCH (EGARCH) models.

All three belong to the GARCH family used to model time-varying conditional variance (volatility) of financial returns, but they differ in what they add to the basic specification.

ModelDescription & distinguishing feature
GARCH(p,q)The baseline model. Conditional variance depends on past squared residuals (ARCH terms) and past conditional variances: ht = ω + α ε²t-1 + β ht-1. It is symmetric — positive and negative shocks of equal size change volatility by the same amount — and requires non-negativity constraints (ω>0, α,β≥0) so that variance stays positive. It captures volatility clustering well but cannot model asymmetry or the risk–return link.
GARCH-M
(GARCH-in-Mean)		Extends GARCH by inserting the conditional variance (or standard deviation) directly into the mean equation: rt = μ + λ ht + εt. This explicitly models the risk–return tradeoff: when λ>0, periods of higher expected volatility carry higher expected returns, compensating investors for bearing risk. The variance equation is the same as GARCH.
EGARCH
(Exponential GARCH)		Models the logarithm of the conditional variance, so variance is guaranteed positive without imposing non-negativity restrictions on the parameters. Crucially, it captures the leverage / asymmetric effect — bad news (negative shocks) raises future volatility more than good news (positive shocks) of the same magnitude, a well-documented feature of equity markets that symmetric GARCH misses.

In summary: GARCH is the symmetric baseline, GARCH-M adds risk into the return equation, and EGARCH adds asymmetry while removing parameter constraints.

7. Describe the concept of volatility forecasting.

Volatility forecasting is the process of predicting the future variability (variance or standard deviation) of asset returns rather than the direction or level of the returns themselves. Because volatility is the primary measure of risk, forecasting it accurately is one of the central tasks in financial analytics.

Basis: forecasting is possible because volatility exhibits clustering and persistence — high-volatility periods tend to follow high-volatility periods — so today's volatility carries information about tomorrow's, even though returns are nearly unpredictable.

Common methods:

  • Historical / rolling standard deviation: a simple moving estimate from past returns.
  • EWMA (exponentially weighted moving average): gives more weight to recent observations.
  • Conditional-variance models: ARCH, GARCH, EGARCH and TGARCH explicitly model time-varying variance and are the workhorses of volatility forecasting.
  • Implied volatility: extracted from option prices, providing a market-based, forward-looking forecast.

Applications: Value-at-Risk and risk management, option pricing, dynamic hedging, portfolio optimisation and position sizing. Forecast quality is judged by comparing predictions against realised volatility using error measures such as RMSE or MAE.

8. What are high-frequency financial data? Explain their characteristics.

High-frequency data (HFD) are financial observations recorded at very short time intervals — seconds, milliseconds, or tick-by-tick, meaning every individual transaction or quote update during the trading day. The rise of electronic and algorithmic trading has made such data central to modern market microstructure analysis.

Characteristics:

  • Irregular spacing and nonsynchronous trading: trades arrive at uneven, random times and different assets trade at different moments, which complicates analysis and biases correlation/covariance estimates.
  • Bid-ask bounce: recorded prices oscillate between the bid and the ask as buyer- and seller-initiated trades alternate, injecting microstructure noise into returns; the bid-ask spread itself is a key observable feature.
  • Volatility clustering: turbulent and calm periods cluster together, producing strong autocorrelation in squared returns even though raw returns show almost none.
  • Fat tails (excess kurtosis): extreme price moves occur far more often than a normal distribution would predict.
  • Intraday seasonality: activity and volatility follow a U-shaped pattern — high near the open and close, lower at midday.
  • Discreteness and huge volume: prices move in discrete ticks and the sheer quantity of data demands specialised storage, cleaning and processing.
9. What is Value at Risk (VaR)? Discuss its importance.

Value at Risk (VaR) is a single summary measure of downside risk that estimates the maximum potential loss of a portfolio over a specified time horizon at a given confidence level. For example, a 1-day 95% VaR of ₹10,000 means there is only a 5% chance that the portfolio will lose more than ₹10,000 in a single day (equivalently, 95% confidence the loss will not exceed ₹10,000).

Methods of estimation:

  • Historical Simulation — re-uses the actual distribution of past returns.
  • Parametric (variance–covariance) — assumes returns are normally distributed and uses the mean and standard deviation (e.g. VaR ≈ z × σ × portfolio value).
  • Monte Carlo Simulation — generates thousands of random scenarios from an assumed model.

Importance:

  • Condenses complex risk into one intuitive number that managers and regulators can understand and compare across desks, assets and institutions.
  • Used to set risk limits, allocate capital and meet regulatory capital requirements under frameworks such as Basel.
  • Supports performance evaluation on a risk-adjusted basis.

Limitation: standard VaR says nothing about the size of losses beyond the threshold and can violate sub-additivity (it may not reward diversification), which is why the coherent measure Expected Shortfall (Tail VaR) is often preferred.

10. Discuss the importance of risk management in financial markets.

Risk management is the systematic process of identifying, measuring, monitoring and controlling the various risks a financial institution or investor faces — including market risk, credit risk, liquidity risk and operational risk. Because financial markets are inherently uncertain, managing risk rather than merely chasing return is fundamental to survival and success.

Why it is important:

  • Protects capital and solvency: by limiting exposures and losses it prevents a single adverse event or extreme market move from causing insolvency or bankruptcy.
  • Regulatory compliance: regulators (Basel Committee, SEBI, RBI) mandate capital buffers, VaR reporting and stress testing; sound risk management ensures the firm meets these obligations and avoids penalties.
  • Financial stability and confidence: at the system level it reduces contagion and systemic risk, and at the firm level it maintains the trust of investors, depositors and counterparties.
  • Better decision-making: risk-adjusted measures (Sharpe ratio, VaR limits, beta) guide capital allocation, position sizing and product pricing so that returns are pursued only for acceptable levels of risk.
  • Tools used: diversification, hedging with derivatives, stress testing and scenario analysis, VaR and Expected Shortfall, and stop-loss limits.
11. Describe a financial time series. Give two examples.

A financial time series is a sequence of financial observations recorded in chronological order at successive (usually regular) time intervals. The defining feature is that the ordering in time matters — successive observations are typically dependent on one another, so the data cannot be treated as an independent random sample. Such series are the raw material of financial analytics and forecasting.

Typical properties: they often display trends, volatility clustering (calm and turbulent periods grouping together), fat tails (frequent extreme moves), and sometimes seasonality. Raw price series are usually non-stationary, while returns are generally closer to stationary.

Examples:

  • Example 1: the daily closing prices (or daily returns) of a stock such as Infosys recorded over a year.
  • Example 2: the monthly USD/INR exchange rate, or a daily series of government bond yields / interest rates.
12. Explain multivariate returns. What is their significance?

Multivariate returns are the returns of several assets observed simultaneously over time and analysed jointly, rather than one series at a time. For example, recording the daily return of every stock in a portfolio produces, on each day, a vector of returns; studied together these form a multivariate return series. This contrasts with univariate analysis, which examines a single asset in isolation.

Significance:

  • Captures co-movement: it reveals the correlations and the variance–covariance structure among assets — how they move together or against each other.
  • Essential for diversification and portfolio construction: portfolio risk depends not only on each asset's own variance but critically on the covariances between assets; combining imperfectly correlated assets reduces total risk.
  • Risk management: joint behaviour matters most during crises, when correlations tend to rise and diversification benefits shrink.
  • Underpins key models: CAPM, multi-factor models and multivariate GARCH all rely on multivariate returns to estimate systematic risk and forecast joint volatility and correlation.
13. Describe the ARMA model with a suitable example.

An ARMA(p,q) model combines two components to describe a stationary time series: an Autoregressive (AR) part, which uses p lagged values of the series, and a Moving Average (MA) part, which uses q lagged random error (shock) terms. By blending both it can represent a wide range of dependence structures parsimoniously (with few parameters). The general form is:

Xt = c + φ1Xt-1 + … + φpXt-p + εt + θ1εt-1 + … + θqεt-q

where the φ's are AR coefficients, the θ's are MA coefficients and εt is white noise.

  • The AR part captures persistence (dependence on past levels) while the MA part captures the lingering effect of past shocks/news.
  • Orders p and q are identified from the ACF and PACF and refined with AIC/BIC.
  • Example — ARMA(1,1): Xt = 0.6 Xt-1 + εt + 0.3 εt-1. Here the current value depends on 60% of the previous value plus the current shock and 30% of the previous shock.
  • For non-stationary data the series is first differenced, extending ARMA to the ARIMA model.
14. Describe the significance of testing for a unit root in time series analysis.

A unit root is a property indicating that a time series is non-stationary — it behaves like a random walk whose mean and variance change over time and in which shocks have permanent (non-decaying) effects. Testing for a unit root therefore amounts to testing whether the series is stationary, which is a crucial preliminary step before any modelling.

Why the test matters:

  • Stationarity is a prerequisite: standard ARMA estimation, inference and forecasting are only valid for stationary data, so the presence of a unit root must be checked first.
  • Avoids spurious regression: regressing one non-stationary series on another can produce a high R² and "significant" coefficients that are entirely meaningless — a classic trap that unit-root testing prevents.
  • The standard test: the Augmented Dickey–Fuller (ADF) test is most commonly used, with the null hypothesis H₀ that a unit root exists (series is non-stationary); related tests include Phillips–Perron and KPSS.
  • Remedy: if a unit root is found, the series is made stationary by differencing (or transformation) before fitting a model — this determines the "I" (order of integration, d) in an ARIMA model.
15. Explain the concept of volatility in financial markets.

Volatility measures the degree of variation or dispersion of an asset's returns around their mean over a period of time. It is most commonly quantified by the standard deviation (or variance) of returns and is the principal statistical proxy for risk in finance: the higher the volatility, the more uncertain and risky the asset.

Types and key features:

  • Historical (realised) volatility is computed from past returns, whereas implied volatility is inferred from current option prices and is forward-looking.
  • Financial volatility exhibits well-known stylised facts: volatility clustering (turbulent periods group together), persistence, mean-reversion over the long run, and the leverage effect (negative shocks raise volatility more than positive ones).
  • Consequences: higher volatility implies greater uncertainty, larger option premiums, wider Value-at-Risk estimates and a need for more active risk management.
  • Modelling: because volatility changes over time, it is modelled with the ARCH/GARCH family rather than assumed constant.
16. What is the ARCH effect? Describe the characteristics of the ARCH model.

The ARCH effect refers to the empirical phenomenon of time-varying, autocorrelated conditional variance in a return series — that is, periods of high volatility tend to cluster together so that large shocks are followed by large shocks. Statistically it shows up as significant autocorrelation in the squared residuals of a model. Its presence is formally checked using Engle's ARCH-LM test, whose null hypothesis is that there is no ARCH effect (constant variance).

The ARCH (Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity) model, introduced by Engle (1982), was the first model to make variance depend on the past.

Characteristics of the ARCH(q) model:

  • The conditional variance is a function of past squared residuals: ht = ω + α1ε²t-1 + … + αqε²t-q.
  • It explicitly captures volatility clustering and the fat-tailed behaviour of returns.
  • The parameters must be non-negative (ω>0, αi≥0) to guarantee a positive variance.
  • It treats volatility as a deterministic function of past shocks (volatility is observable given the past).
  • Limitation: capturing realistic persistence often requires a large number of lags (q), making the model unwieldy — this motivated the more parsimonious GARCH model.
17. Explain the concept of volatility clustering.

Volatility clustering is a stylised fact of financial returns: large price changes tend to be followed by large changes (of either sign), and small changes by small changes. In other words, high-volatility (turbulent) periods and low-volatility (calm) periods each group together over time rather than being randomly scattered — "volatility breeds volatility".

Key points:

  • It implies that, although the direction of returns is almost unpredictable, the magnitude of returns (volatility) is persistent and partly predictable.
  • Statistically, it appears as significant positive autocorrelation in squared or absolute returns, while the autocorrelation of raw returns is close to zero.
  • It is often visible directly in a time-series plot of returns as alternating quiet and turbulent stretches.
  • It is the central empirical motivation for ARCH and GARCH conditional-variance models, which are specifically designed to capture and forecast it.
18. What is nonsynchronous trading?

Nonsynchronous (asynchronous) trading occurs when different assets are traded at different points in time, so that their last recorded prices are not measured at the same instant even though they are grouped into the same time interval (e.g. the same "closing" snapshot). It is also called the non-trading or stale-price problem.

Key points:

  • Cause: assets differ in liquidity — a heavily traded stock updates every second, while a thinly traded one may not trade for minutes or hours, leaving a stale last price.
  • Effects on analysis: it induces spurious autocorrelation in individual return series and biases (typically under-estimates) the cross-correlations, covariances and betas measured between assets, because their prices reflect information from slightly different times.
  • Relevance: it is especially important in high-frequency data and in portfolio/lead-lag studies, where uncorrected nonsynchronous trading distorts risk and relationship estimates; adjustments are made to recover accurate covariance and beta estimates.
19. What is the Peak Over Threshold (POT) method?

The Peak Over Threshold (POT) method is an approach from Extreme Value Theory (EVT) used to model the tail of a distribution — that is, the behaviour of rare, extreme losses. Instead of looking at the whole distribution, it focuses only on the observations that exceed a chosen high threshold u.

How it works:

  • A high threshold u is selected, and only the values above it are retained. The amounts by which they exceed the threshold — the excesses (X − u, for X > u) — are then analysed.
  • By the Pickands–Balkema–de Haan theorem, these excesses are modelled by the Generalised Pareto Distribution (GPD).
  • The fitted tail is then used to estimate extreme risk measures such as Value-at-Risk and Expected Shortfall at very high confidence levels.

Advantages and trade-offs: POT uses data more efficiently than the alternative block-maxima/GEV approach because it employs every observation that exceeds the threshold, not just one maximum per block. The main practical challenge is choosing the threshold: too low introduces bias (non-tail data contaminate the fit), while too high leaves too few observations and high variance.

20. What is meant by coherence in risk measurement?

Coherence is a set of mathematical conditions, proposed by Artzner, Delbaen, Eber and Heath (1999), that a "sensible" or well-behaved risk measure ought to satisfy. A risk measure ρ is called coherent if it satisfies all four axioms below:

  • Monotonicity: if portfolio A always produces outcomes at least as bad as portfolio B, then A must carry at least as much risk — worse positions cannot be assigned lower risk.
  • Subadditivity: ρ(A + B) ≤ ρ(A) + ρ(B). Combining two portfolios cannot create more risk than the sum of the parts — this formalises the benefit of diversification.
  • Positive homogeneity: ρ(λA) = λ ρ(A) for λ ≥ 0 — scaling all positions up or down scales the risk proportionally.
  • Translation invariance: adding a risk-free amount of cash c to a portfolio reduces its risk by exactly c — ρ(A + c) = ρ(A) − c.

Importance: coherence guarantees the measure behaves consistently and rewards diversification. VaR is not coherent because it can violate subadditivity (the VaR of a combined portfolio can exceed the sum of individual VaRs), whereas Expected Shortfall (ES / Tail VaR) is coherent, which is a major reason regulators have moved towards it.

21. Describe an asset return. Why is it preferred over asset price in financial analysis?

An asset return measures the gain or loss on an investment relative to its value over a holding period, usually expressed as a percentage. The simple (net) return between t−1 and t is:

Rt = (Pt − Pt-1) / Pt-1

and the continuously compounded (log) return is rt = ln(Pt / Pt-1). Returns may also include income such as dividends or coupons.

Why returns are preferred over prices:

  • Scale-free and comparable: returns remove the effect of the absolute price level, so assets trading at very different prices (e.g. a ₹50 stock and a ₹5,000 stock) can be compared directly.
  • Better statistical properties: price series are typically non-stationary (they wander like a random walk), whereas return series are generally much closer to stationary, which is required for most time-series models and valid statistical inference.
  • Convenient aggregation: log returns are additive over time (multi-period return = sum of single-period log returns), simplifying analysis.
  • Returns are the natural input to portfolio theory, risk models and pricing models, which are all expressed in terms of expected return and the variance/covariance of returns.
22. Explain the bond yield. What does it represent?

A bond yield is the rate of return an investor earns on a bond, expressed as an annual percentage. Several yield measures exist depending on what is included:

  • Coupon rate: the fixed annual coupon as a percentage of the bond's face value.
  • Current yield: the annual coupon divided by the bond's current market price — a quick measure of income relative to price.
  • Yield to Maturity (YTM): the single discount rate that makes the present value of all the bond's future coupon payments and its principal repayment equal to its current market price. YTM is the most complete measure — it represents the total annualised return an investor would earn if the bond is held to maturity and all coupons are reinvested at that rate.

What it represents: the yield reflects the bond's effective rate of return and, more broadly, the return the market currently requires to hold a bond of that maturity and credit risk. A crucial relationship is the inverse link between price and yield: when market interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall (so their yields rise), and when rates fall, prices rise (yields fall).

23. Explain the moving average (MA) model with an example.

A Moving Average MA(q) model is a linear time-series model in which the current value of a series is expressed as a constant (its mean) plus a weighted combination of the current and past random error (shock) terms. In other words, the present value is driven by recent unexpected "news" rather than by past values of the series itself. The general form is:

Xt = μ + εt + θ1εt-1 + … + θqεt-q

where μ is the mean, εt is white noise, the θ's are MA coefficients and q is the order.

  • It captures the effect of past shocks/news that influence the series for q periods and then disappear — giving MA processes a short, finite memory.
  • The appropriate order q is identified from the autocorrelation function (ACF), which cuts off after lag q.
  • Example — MA(1): Xt = εt + 0.4 εt-1. Today's value is affected fully by today's shock and by 40% of yesterday's shock; a shock two periods ago has no effect at all.
  • Note: this econometric MA model is conceptually different from the simple moving-average smoother that just averages recent prices.
24. Explain the role of stationarity in a financial time series.

A time series is stationary when its key statistical properties do not change over time. Under the common definition of weak (covariance) stationarity this means a constant mean, constant variance, and an autocovariance that depends only on the lag between observations, not on the actual point in time. Stationarity is one of the most important concepts in time-series analysis.

Its role / importance:

  • Foundation for modelling: standard models such as AR, MA and ARMA are built on the assumption of stationarity; reliable estimation, hypothesis testing and forecasting all depend on it.
  • Prevents spurious results: applying regression to non-stationary series can yield spurious relationships — apparently strong and significant links that are in fact meaningless.
  • Stable forecasts: because the statistical structure is constant, patterns learned from history can be projected into the future with confidence.
  • Testing and remedies: stationarity is checked using the ADF and KPSS tests and the ACF plot; a non-stationary series is usually made stationary by differencing or transformation (e.g. taking logs).
  • In practice, raw price levels are typically non-stationary, whereas returns are usually stationary — another reason returns are modelled rather than prices.
25. Explain the concept of seasonality with a suitable example.

Seasonality refers to a pattern in a time series that repeats at fixed, regular intervals — such as daily, weekly, monthly or quarterly — driven by calendar effects, business cycles or human behaviour. It is distinct from a long-term trend (a sustained rise or fall) and from random fluctuations; seasonality is the predictable, recurring component.

Key points:

  • The repetition is described by the seasonal period s: for monthly data s = 12, for quarterly data s = 4, and for daily-within-week data s = 7.
  • Seasonality is identified through time plots, seasonal sub-series plots and a correlogram showing spikes at the seasonal lag.
  • It is handled by seasonal differencing (to remove it) or modelled explicitly with SARIMA or Holt–Winters exponential smoothing, which include a dedicated seasonal component.
  • Example: a retailer's sales rising every December because of festive/holiday demand, or — in financial markets — trading volume and volatility being systematically higher near the market open and close each day.
26. Describe the characteristics of the GARCH model.

The GARCH (Generalised Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity) model, introduced by Bollerslev (1986), generalises Engle's ARCH model by letting the conditional variance depend on its own past values as well as past squared shocks. A GARCH(1,1), the most widely used version, is written:

ht = ω + α ε²t-1 + β ht-1

where ω is a constant, α captures the reaction to the most recent shock, and β captures the persistence of past variance.

Characteristics:

  • It models time-varying conditional variance and captures volatility clustering and fat tails parsimoniously — GARCH(1,1) is usually sufficient where ARCH would need many lags.
  • Persistence is measured by α + β; a value close to 1 means shocks to volatility decay slowly and volatility is highly persistent.
  • Parameter restrictions: ω > 0 and α, β ≥ 0 ensure variance stays positive, and α + β < 1 is required for a finite, stable unconditional (long-run) variance equal to ω / (1 − α − β).
  • It implies mean-reversion of volatility towards its long-run average.
  • Limitation: standard GARCH is symmetric — it treats positive and negative shocks identically and so cannot capture the leverage effect, which asymmetric extensions such as EGARCH and TGARCH address.
27. Describe the concept of the minimum variance portfolio.

The minimum variance portfolio (MVP) is the particular combination of assets that achieves the lowest possible portfolio risk (variance or standard deviation), irrespective of the level of expected return. Graphically it is the leftmost point of the efficient frontier — no other portfolio of the same assets has a smaller variance.

Key points:

  • Its composition is driven by the assets' variances and, crucially, their covariances/correlations — not simply by picking the individually least-risky assets.
  • It exploits diversification: combining assets that are imperfectly (or negatively) correlated reduces overall risk below the weighted average of the individual risks.
  • Mathematically it is found by minimising the portfolio variance wTΣw subject to the weights summing to one (and possibly no-short-selling constraints), where Σ is the variance–covariance matrix.
  • It is particularly attractive to highly risk-averse investors who value stability over maximising returns, and serves as a benchmark in portfolio optimisation.
28. Explain the bid-ask spread. What does it represent?

The bid-ask spread is the difference between the best ask price (the lowest price at which a seller is willing to sell) and the best bid price (the highest price at which a buyer is willing to buy):

Spread = Ask − Bid

For example, if the bid is ₹200 and the ask is ₹202, the spread is ₹2.

What it represents:

  • Transaction cost: it is the implicit cost of trading immediately — a trader who buys at the ask and sells at the bid loses the spread.
  • Market liquidity: a narrow spread signals high liquidity (many buyers and sellers, easy to trade), while a wide spread signals low liquidity and higher cost/risk.
  • Compensation for market makers: dealers who continuously quote bids and asks earn the spread as payment for providing liquidity and bearing inventory risk.
  • Source of microstructure noise: as trades alternate between hitting the bid and the ask, prices exhibit bid-ask bounce, which injects noise into high-frequency returns.
29. Differentiate between simple return and log return.

Both measure the return on an asset between two dates, but they are defined and behave differently. The simple (arithmetic) return measures the proportional change in price, while the log (continuously compounded) return is the natural logarithm of the price ratio.

30. What is meant by time-varying beta?

Beta measures an asset's systematic (market) risk — its sensitivity to movements in the overall market — and is the key parameter of the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). A beta of 1.2, for instance, means the asset tends to move 1.2% for every 1% move in the market. The traditional CAPM assumes beta is a fixed constant.

Time-varying beta recognises that this assumption is unrealistic: an asset's market sensitivity changes over time.

  • Why it varies: beta shifts with the business cycle, with changes in a firm's leverage, size and fundamentals, and especially during market crises, when correlations and risk exposures rise sharply.
  • Importance: assuming a constant beta misstates risk and leads to mispriced assets and poorly sized hedges; allowing beta to vary gives more accurate, up-to-date estimates of risk and required return.
  • Estimation: time-varying beta is estimated using rolling-window regressions, state-space models with the Kalman filter, or multivariate GARCH models that compute beta as the ratio of the conditional covariance with the market to the market's conditional variance.
