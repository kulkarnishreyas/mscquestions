① Data Visualisation Principles

Effective data visualisation is both a science and an art. Before building any chart, ask: who is reading this, what decision do they need to make, and what data supports that decision? The 10 principles below form the foundation of every great dashboard and every examiner's checklist.

1

Know Your Audience — Technical vs Non-Technical

A data scientist can read a residual plot; a marketing director cannot. Tailor your chart complexity, jargon, and level of annotation to the viewer. For executives: big numbers, minimal axes, clear takeaway titles. For analysts: full axis labels, underlying data tables, confidence intervals. Always ask: what does this person need to DO after seeing this chart?

2

Choose the Right Chart Type — Data Type Determines Chart

The cardinal rule: the data type you have dictates which chart you should use. Categorical data → bar chart. Time series → line chart. Distribution → histogram or box plot. Part-to-whole → pie or treemap. Using the wrong chart (e.g., a pie chart for a trend) is not just aesthetically wrong — it actively misleads the viewer. Chart selection is covered in full in Section 2.

3

Simplicity — Remove Chart Junk (Tufte's Data-Ink Ratio)

Edward Tufte coined the term chart junk to describe all the visual elements in a chart that do not convey information: decorative borders, 3D effects, unnecessary gridlines, redundant legends, gradient fills, and clip art. His principle of the data-ink ratio states:

Data-Ink Ratio = Data Ink / Total Ink Used in the Graphic

A ratio close to 1 means almost every pixel of ink represents data. A good rule: after you build a chart, try deleting every element one by one. If removing it doesn't lose information, remove it permanently. Remove backgrounds, 3D effects, excess gridlines, and redundant legends first.

4

Colour Purposefully — Max 5–7 Colours, Contrast, Colour-Blind Friendly

Colour should encode meaning, not decorate. Use a sequential palette (light → dark) for ordered/continuous data. Use a diverging palette (two hues meeting at a neutral midpoint) for data that diverges from zero or a target. Use a qualitative palette (distinct hues) for categorical comparisons — but limit to 5–7 categories; beyond that, the eye cannot distinguish them reliably.

  • Colour-blind safety: ~8% of males have red-green colour blindness (deuteranopia). Avoid red/green together without shape or label backup. Use tools like ColorBrewer or Tableau's built-in colour-blind palettes.
  • Contrast: ensure WCAG AA contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for text on colour backgrounds.
  • Highlight colour: use your accent colour (e.g., orange) only for the single most important data point — don't spread it everywhere.
5

Label Clearly — Axis Labels, Titles, Units Always Present

Every chart must have: (a) a descriptive title that states the insight, not just the topic — "Revenue Fell 18% in Q3" not "Q3 Revenue"; (b) both axis labels with units (₹ thousands, %, days); (c) a source line at the bottom. In Tableau, always edit the axis title — the default field name is rarely human-readable. For direct labelling (labels on bars/lines instead of a legend), the reader's eye doesn't have to travel across the chart.

6

Highlight What Matters — Call-Outs, Annotations, Reference Lines

If a chart requires the reader to hunt for the insight, the chart has failed. Use reference lines (e.g., target line, average line) to give context. Use annotations to explain outliers or sudden changes ("Product recall" next to a dip). In Tableau, reference lines are added via the Analytics pane. Highlight the single key data point with a different colour or bold label — everything else recedes.

7

Show Context — Comparisons, Benchmarks, Targets

A single number is meaningless without context. "Sales = ₹4.2 Cr" means nothing. "Sales = ₹4.2 Cr vs target ₹5 Cr (–16%)" tells a story. Always show: prior period comparison (YoY, MoM), a benchmark (industry average, competitor), or a target. Bullet charts are excellent for showing a value vs a target vs a qualitative range (poor/satisfactory/good).

8

Tell a Story — Narrative Arc: Beginning / Middle / End

Data visualisation at its best is storytelling with numbers. A narrative arc means: Beginning — establish the business context and the question; Middle — present the evidence (charts, trends, comparisons) that builds the case; End — deliver the insight and the recommended action. Dashboards that dump all charts simultaneously without a flow force the viewer to construct their own narrative — which they rarely do correctly. Use Tableau Storyboards to enforce the narrative sequence.

9

Interactivity — Filters, Drill-Downs, Tooltips Add Value

Static charts are appropriate for printed reports. Digital dashboards should be interactive. Interactivity rules: (a) make filters visible and clearly labelled; (b) tooltips should show the exact value + any additional context, not just a repetition of what's already visible; (c) drill-down (clicking a region to see city-level data) reduces the need for dozens of separate charts; (d) don't add interactivity that doesn't serve a decision — every extra filter adds cognitive load. In Tableau, Dashboard Actions (Filter, Highlight, URL) drive interactivity.

10

Accuracy — Don't Truncate Axes to Mislead

The most common form of chart manipulation is truncating the y-axis — starting it at a value other than zero. A bar chart that starts at 95 makes a 1-unit difference look enormous. The rule: bar charts must start at zero because bar length encodes value. Line charts may use a non-zero baseline when the absolute value range is small and the trend is the focus — but always label clearly. Never use 3D charts; the perspective distorts relative areas. Never use dual-axis charts unless both axes are clearly labelled with different scales and colours.

② Variable Types & Chart Selection

The single most important skill in data visualisation is mapping your data type to the correct chart type. This section gives you a systematic framework: identify the measurement level of your variable(s), identify the relationship you want to show, then select from the appropriate chart family.

Measurement Levels

Nominal (Categorical, No Order)

Categories with no inherent ranking. Examples: store location (Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore), product category (Coffee, Tea, Pastry), payment method.

Recommended charts:

  • Bar chart — compare frequencies or values across categories
  • Pie/Donut chart — part-to-whole when ≤5 categories
  • Treemap — part-to-whole with hierarchical nesting
  • Word cloud — frequency of text items (informal use only)

Ordinal (Categorical, With Order)

Categories with a meaningful rank but unequal intervals. Examples: satisfaction rating (Poor/Fair/Good/Excellent), education level, age group (18–24, 25–34, 35+).

Recommended charts:

  • Ordered bar chart — bars arranged by rank, not alphabetically
  • Likert scale visualisation — diverging stacked bars (Negative ← | → Positive)
  • Dot plot — compact comparison of ranked categories

Continuous / Ratio Data (Numeric)

Measured values with equal intervals and a true zero. Examples: revenue (₹), temperature (°C), weight (kg), age in years, profit margin (%).

Recommended charts:

  • Histogram — shape of distribution (bins)
  • Box plot — 5-number summary + outliers
  • Line chart — trend over continuous variable (usually time)
  • Scatter plot — relationship between two continuous variables

Time Series (Temporal)

Data recorded at successive points or intervals in time. Examples: daily revenue, monthly active users, weekly stock price, quarterly growth.

Recommended charts:

  • Line chart — the standard for continuous time series
  • Area chart — emphasises cumulative volume over time
  • Stacked area — shows composition changing over time
  • Bar chart — acceptable for discrete periods (months/quarters)

Special Chart Notes

Box Plot — 5-Number Summary

A box plot shows: Minimum, Q1 (25th percentile), Median (Q2), Q3 (75th percentile), Maximum. The IQR (Inter-Quartile Range = Q3 − Q1) forms the box. Whiskers extend to 1.5 × IQR beyond the box. Points beyond whiskers are plotted individually as outliers. Use when you need to compare distributions across multiple groups and detect outliers simultaneously.

Choropleth vs Symbol Map

Choropleth map: fills geographic regions with colour intensity to show rates or ratios (e.g., GDP per capita by state, vaccination rate by district). Best for normalised data. Symbol map: places sized circles or shapes at geographic points to show absolute values (e.g., store revenue by city). Avoid choropleth for raw totals — large areas appear dominant even with small values.

③ Tableau Products

Tableau (acquired by Salesforce in 2019) is a suite of business intelligence and analytics products. Understanding which product to use for which scenario is a common exam and interview question. The products span from free public sharing to enterprise on-premise governance.

Tableau Desktop

Paid

The primary authoring tool. Connects to 70+ data sources (Excel, CSV, SQL databases, cloud data warehouses like Snowflake/BigQuery, web connectors). Creates .twb (workbook) or .twbx (packaged workbook with data) files. Supports calculated fields, parameters, LOD expressions, and all chart types. Available as Creator or Explorer license. Runs on Windows and macOS.

Tableau Server

Paid · On-Premise

Enterprise on-premise deployment for sharing and governing Tableau content within an organisation. Features: role-based access control, row-level security, scheduled data refreshes, version control, subscription emails, REST API for embedding. IT team manages the server hardware and software. Requires significant infrastructure investment. Best for organisations with strict data residency requirements.

Tableau Online (Cloud)

Paid · SaaS

Cloud-hosted version of Tableau Server managed by Salesforce. Same features as Server but no infrastructure to maintain. Connects to cloud data sources natively. Suitable for organisations that want Server capabilities without IT overhead. Data lives in Salesforce's cloud — not suitable for highly sensitive data in some jurisdictions.

Tableau Public

Free

Free version for creating and publishing visualisations to the public Tableau Public gallery. Key limitation: all workbooks are publicly visible — no private data. Data limit of 15 million rows. No scheduling. Used by journalists, data enthusiasts, and students for portfolios. Cannot connect to private databases. Download workbooks are freely available to any visitor.

Tableau Prep

Paid (with Desktop)

Visual ETL (Extract-Transform-Load) tool for data cleaning and shaping before analysis. Features: join, union, pivot, split, clean (fix nulls, standardise casing, remove duplicates), create calculated fields, and output to Tableau Desktop or published data sources. The visual flow diagram makes it easy to document the data preparation pipeline. Replaces much of what used to require SQL or Python for data cleaning.

Tableau Mobile & Reader

Free (Mobile)Free (Reader)

Tableau Mobile: iOS/Android app for consuming Tableau Server/Online dashboards on phones and tablets. Optimised touch interface with offline support. Tableau Reader: Free desktop application to view and interact with packaged workbooks (.twbx) published by Tableau Desktop. Cannot create or edit — view only. Useful for sharing with clients who don't have a Tableau license.

Advanced Analytics in Tableau

Beyond static charts, Tableau supports complex calculations, trend analysis, forecasting, and statistical functions — enabling in-depth data analysis and interpretation. These advanced features support predictive and prescriptive analytics by helping forecast future trends and make strategic recommendations directly inside the visualisation workflow.

④ Tableau Interface — Measures vs Dimensions

Understanding Tableau's interface and data model is fundamental to working efficiently. The most important concept is the distinction between Dimensions and Measures — and the secondary distinction between Discrete and Continuous fields.

Dimensions vs Measures

Dimensions Blue Pills

Dimensions are categorical, qualitative fields. They slice and dice the data — they define the level of detail at which your measures are aggregated. When you place a Dimension on a shelf, Tableau creates a header (a label).

Examples: Region, Product Name, Customer ID, Order Date (when discrete), Country, Category, Customer Segment, Sales Rep Name.

  • Shown in the top section of the Data pane
  • Default aggregation: none (they group, not aggregate)
  • Placed on Rows/Columns to create row/column headers
  • Used in Filters to include/exclude categories

Measures Green Pills

Measures are numeric, quantitative fields. They are the values you want to aggregate and analyse. When you place a Measure on a shelf, Tableau creates an axis.

Examples: Sales (₹), Profit (₹), Quantity (units), Discount (%), Revenue, Customer Count, Margin %.

  • Shown in the bottom section of the Data pane
  • Default aggregation: SUM (can change to AVG, MIN, MAX, COUNT, etc.)
  • Placed on Rows/Columns to create continuous axes
  • Used in the Marks card for Size, Colour, Label, Tooltip

Discrete vs Continuous

Discrete (Blue) — Creates Headers

Discrete fields have a finite set of distinct values. When placed on a shelf, they partition the view into distinct sections/headers. A Dimension is always discrete by default. A Measure can be made discrete (right-click → Convert to Discrete) — it will then show as a label rather than an axis.

Visual cue: Blue pill in the view. Creates labels: "North", "South", "East", "West".

Continuous (Green) — Creates Axes

Continuous fields form an unbroken range of values. When placed on a shelf, they create a continuous axis. A Measure is continuous by default. A Dimension (like Date) can be made continuous (right-click → Convert to Continuous) — it will then show as a date axis rather than discrete headers.

Visual cue: Green pill in the view. Creates axis: 0 → 100,000 → 200,000.

Calculated Fields

Calculated fields let you create new fields using Tableau's formula language. They appear in the Data pane with an equals sign (=) and can be Dimensions or Measures depending on their output.

/* Calculated Field Examples in Tableau */ // Profit Margin % (Measure) Profit Margin = (SUM([Revenue]) - SUM([Cost])) / SUM([Revenue]) * 100 // Sales Tier (Dimension — creates categories) IF [Sales] > 10000 THEN "High" ELSEIF [Sales] > 5000 THEN "Medium" ELSE "Low" END // YoY Growth (Table Calculation) ([Sales] - LOOKUP([Sales], -1)) / ABS(LOOKUP([Sales], -1)) * 100 // Level of Detail (LOD) Expression // Average sales per customer regardless of current view level {FIXED [Customer ID] : AVG([Sales])} // Date calculation DATEDIFF('day', [Order Date], [Ship Date])
Key Distinction — LOD Expressions: LOD (Level of Detail) expressions use {FIXED}, {INCLUDE}, or {EXCLUDE} to control which dimensions are used for the calculation, independent of the view's current level of detail. For example, {FIXED [Region] : SUM([Sales])} always computes sales at the Region level, even if the view is at City level.

⑤ Chart Type Selection Rules

Beyond knowing chart categories, experienced analysts follow a set of practical decision rules that prevent the most common visualisation mistakes. Memorise these seven rules — they cover the vast majority of business visualisation scenarios.

Rule 1 — Comparing Categories → Horizontal Bar Chart

When comparing values across categories (stores, products, regions, sales reps), use a horizontal bar chart rather than vertical. Why horizontal? Long category labels (e.g., "Specialty Coffee Beverages", "Cold Brew Nitro") fit naturally on the left axis without rotation. Sort bars in descending order so the viewer's eye follows the ranking naturally from top to bottom. Use a vertical bar chart only when the category labels are very short (Jan, Feb, Mar) or when you want to emphasise the height metaphor.

Rule 2 — Trend Over Time → Line Chart (Not Bar)

Time series data should almost always be a line chart. Lines visually encode continuity and connect data points across time, which is the correct mental model — time flows continuously. A bar chart for time series implies that each period is independent and discrete. Reserve bar charts for time data only when you have a small number of discrete periods (e.g., annual comparison of 3 years) and you want to emphasise the absolute magnitude of each year rather than the trend.

Rule 3 — Part-to-Whole → Pie only if <5 Segments; else Stacked Bar

Pie charts work when: (a) there are 5 or fewer segments, (b) the segments are meaningfully different in size, and (c) the exact percentages are labelled. Beyond 5 segments, the slices become indistinguishable, and a stacked bar chart (or treemap) is far more readable. Donut charts (pie with centre removed) are acceptable when you want to place a KPI number in the centre. Never use multiple pie charts side by side for comparison — grouped bar charts are always better for comparison across categories.

Rule 4 — Distribution → Box Plot for Outliers; Histogram for Shape

Histogram: use when you want to understand the shape of a distribution — is it normal, skewed, bimodal? Group continuous data into bins and show frequency. Box plot: use when you want to compare distributions across groups AND identify outliers. A box plot for "Revenue by Store" lets you see at a glance which store has the most variability and which stores have outlier days. Violin plot: combines box plot with kernel density — shows the full distribution shape — available in R/Python but not natively in Tableau.

Rule 5 — Two-Variable Relationship → Scatter Plot; Add Size for 3rd Variable (Bubble Chart)

A scatter plot encodes two continuous variables on x and y axes. Each point is an observation. Use it to discover correlation, clusters, and outliers. Add a trend line (linear or polynomial) to show the direction of the relationship. For a bubble chart, a third variable is encoded as the size of each bubble — for example: x = Units Sold, y = Revenue, size = Profit Margin. Limit bubble charts to ~20 data points or they become unreadable. Colour can encode a fourth variable (categorical).

Rule 6 — Geographic Data → Map (Choropleth for Rates; Symbol for Values)

Choropleth map: colour-fill regions (countries, states, districts) to show normalised rates or percentages (revenue per capita, market penetration %). Symbol map (proportional symbol): place sized circles at geographic points to show absolute values (total revenue by city). The key distinction: choropleths can mislead when large geographic areas dominate the visual even with small data values (e.g., a small but populous city vs a large but sparse rural state). Always use rates for choropleths, absolute values for symbols.

Rule 7 — KPI Summary → Big Number + Sparkline

For executive dashboards, the most valuable element is often a large, bold number with a sparkline (a tiny trend line) below it and a comparison delta (e.g., ▲ 12% vs last month). This big number + sparkline combination gives the current value, the trend direction, and the magnitude of change in a single glance — no axes needed, no gridlines, maximum data-ink ratio.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ 3D Charts

3D bar, 3D pie, and 3D line charts introduce perspective distortion that makes it impossible to accurately read values. The tilt creates an illusion where front bars appear larger than back bars of equal height. 3D effects add ink without adding data — maximum violation of Tufte's principle. Never use 3D charts in professional work.

❌ Dual-Axis Misuse

Dual-axis charts (two y-axes on one chart) can show two different measures together, but are frequently misused to imply correlation where none exists. If the second axis scale is manipulated, the lines can be made to cross or diverge at will. Rule: use dual-axis only when both measures are genuinely related and both axes are clearly labelled with their scales and units.

❌ Pie Charts with Many Slices

Pie charts with 6+ slices require a legend, forcing the viewer's eye to travel back and forth between the legend and the chart. Slices of similar size (e.g., 16% vs 14% vs 13%) are indistinguishable. The human eye is poor at comparing angles and areas. Replace with a sorted horizontal bar chart and label the bars directly.

❌ Rainbow Colour Scales

Using a rainbow/spectral colour scale for continuous data (e.g., a heatmap going from violet → red through all spectral colours) implies that there are sharp boundaries between categories. Sequential data (low to high) should use a single-hue scale (light → dark). Diverging data (negative to positive) should use two hues meeting at a neutral grey or white midpoint.

⑥ Dashboard Design — 8 Steps

A Tableau dashboard is a collection of views, filters, and other elements assembled on a single canvas. A great dashboard answers a specific question for a specific audience in under 30 seconds. The 8-step process below is a professional framework for designing dashboards from scratch.

1

Define Audience and Purpose

Who will use this dashboard? What role do they have? What decisions do they make daily? A sales manager needs real-time territory performance vs target. A CFO needs high-level P&L with drill-down capability. A supply chain analyst needs inventory levels and reorder alerts. Write a one-sentence purpose statement: "This dashboard enables [role] to [action] by showing [metric] compared to [benchmark]."

2

Identify Key Questions to Answer

List the 3–5 business questions the dashboard must answer. Examples: "Which product categories are below target this month?", "Which regions show declining revenue trends?", "What is today's conversion rate vs the 30-day average?". Every chart on the dashboard should answer at least one of these questions. If a chart doesn't answer a listed question, remove it.

3

Choose 3–5 Key Metrics (KPIs)

Less is more. A dashboard with 20 metrics overwhelms and paralyses. A dashboard with 3–5 carefully chosen KPIs drives decisions. Prioritise metrics that are: (a) actionable — the viewer can act on them immediately, (b) comparable — can be measured against a target or prior period, (c) current — refreshed at the right cadence (daily, weekly, monthly). Common KPIs for marketing dashboards: Revenue vs Target, Conversion Rate, Customer Acquisition Cost, ROAS, Net Promoter Score.

4

Sketch Layout on Paper First

Before opening Tableau, sketch the dashboard on paper or a whiteboard. Decide: where does the header go? Where are the KPI tiles? Is the main visualisation top-left or centre? Where are the filters? Paper sketching is fast, cheap, and ensures agreement before any build time is invested. Use the F-pattern reading pattern (see below) to guide layout decisions.

5

Build in Tableau: Containers, Layout, Padding

In Tableau, dashboard layout is controlled via containers (horizontal and vertical layout containers) and floating objects. Best practice: use tiled layout with containers rather than floating — it resizes better across screen sizes. Key elements to add: Title text object (top), KPI tiles in a horizontal container, main visualisations in the centre, filter sidebar on the right. Set consistent padding (8–12px) and use a colour-coded background for the header section to create visual hierarchy.

6

Add Filters, Parameters, Actions for Interactivity

Filters: quick filters (dropdown, radio, date range) allow the viewer to slice data. Apply filters to all sheets using the same data source. Parameters: allow the viewer to select a value that drives a calculation — e.g., a "Target Sales" parameter that feeds into a reference line. Dashboard Actions: Filter actions (click a bar to filter all other charts), Highlight actions (hover to highlight related marks), URL actions (click to open a drill-down report or external link). Actions make dashboards feel like apps, not static charts.

7

Test with Real Users — A/B the Layout

Show the dashboard to 3–5 representative users before deploying. Ask them to find specific answers (e.g., "Which region had the highest revenue last month?"). Time how long it takes. If they can't find the answer in under 30 seconds, redesign. A/B test two layout options with different users. Collect feedback on: (a) clarity of labels, (b) intuitiveness of filters, (c) colour meaning, (d) missing context. Iteration is faster than trying to design perfectly on the first attempt.

8

Deploy and Schedule Refresh

Publish to Tableau Server or Online. Set up an extract refresh schedule — daily, hourly, or real-time (live connection). Configure subscription emails to deliver a dashboard snapshot to stakeholders every Monday morning. Set up data-driven alerts (e.g., email if conversion rate drops below 2%). Monitor usage with Tableau's built-in usage analytics — if nobody views a dashboard after 30 days, it should be archived or redesigned.

Dashboard Best Practices

F-Pattern Reading

Eye-tracking research shows users scan digital content in an F-pattern: they read fully across the top, partially across below, then scan down the left side. Place your most important KPIs and the primary chart in the top-left quadrant. Navigation and filters on the right. Secondary details at the bottom. Never put your most important insight at the bottom-right — few users will scroll that far.

Above-the-Fold KPIs

"Above the fold" (borrowed from newspaper design) means the content visible without scrolling. On a standard laptop screen (1366×768), the top ~500px is above the fold. Your 3–5 KPI tiles must be in this zone. The user should be able to assess the health of the business within 5 seconds — before interacting, before scrolling, before reading labels.

3-Click Rule

Any insight a user is looking for should be reachable within 3 clicks or interactions from the dashboard's default state. If it takes more than 3 clicks to drill from "total revenue" to "revenue by product by city by week", the hierarchy is too deep. Redesign with better filters or break into multiple linked dashboards. The 3-click rule ensures dashboards are exploratory tools, not mazes.

⑦ Storyboards — 8 Steps

Tableau Stories (storyboards) are a sequence of worksheets and dashboards arranged to guide the audience through a narrative. Unlike a dashboard (which presents multiple views simultaneously for exploration), a story controls the order of revelation — each story point builds on the last, leading to a conclusion or recommendation.

8 Steps to Build a Tableau Story

1

Define the Story Narrative

What is the central message? State it in one sentence before building anything: "Despite revenue growth, profit margins have declined 5 points over 12 months due to rising input costs in the Coffee category." Every story point must serve this central message. If a chart doesn't advance the argument, it doesn't belong in the story.

2

Identify Data Insights That Support the Story

Before building visuals, write down the 5–8 facts from your data that prove the narrative. Example: "Coffee revenue +22% YoY", "Coffee cost +31% YoY", "Net Coffee margin: 18% → 12%", "Other categories stable". These facts become the content of your story points. If you can't find supporting data, the narrative needs revision.

3

Sequence Story Points Logically

The most effective structure is the problem-solution arc: (1) establish the business context, (2) show the problem clearly, (3) dig into root causes, (4) present the solution or recommendation. Alternative structures: chronological (what happened over time) or comparison (before vs after). Avoid starting with methodology or data sources — start with the outcome the audience cares about.

4

Create Individual Worksheets for Each Story Point

Each story point is typically one worksheet or one dashboard. Build each view independently and optimise it for its specific message. A story point showing "Revenue trend" should have the trend line, a reference annotation at the key inflection point, and a descriptive title stating the insight. Don't reuse a generic dashboard — create a focused view for each argument.

5

Assemble Story in Tableau Story Feature

In Tableau Desktop, click Story → New Story. Drag worksheets or dashboards from the Story pane on the left into each story point. The story navigator (tabs, arrows, or dots) appears at the bottom or top. Set the story size to match your presentation screen (typically 1200×800 for widescreen presentations). Add a title to the story itself.

6

Add Captions and Annotations

Each story point has a caption box at the top (editable text). Write a one-sentence insight statement, not a description — "Coffee margins fell from 24% to 15% as bean costs rose 31%" not "This chart shows coffee margin and cost trends". Add annotations directly on charts (via the worksheet) to point to the key moment: the month costs spiked, the product that underperforms.

7

Add Navigator (Dots / Arrows / Tabs)

Tableau offers three navigator styles: tabs (show all story point titles), dots (minimal, clean — good for presentations), and arrows (next/previous — forces linear progression). Choose based on context: tabs for self-service exploration (audience can jump around), arrows for a live presentation (you control the pace), dots for embedded stories on a web page.

8

Present and Iterate

Rehearse the story verbally while clicking through story points. Each point should take 30–60 seconds to explain. Total story: 5–8 minutes. After presenting, collect feedback: did the audience follow the argument? Did the conclusion surprise them positively or confuse them? Update captions, reorder story points, or add/remove views based on audience reaction. A story is a living document — iterate after each presentation.

Story Types

Data-Driven Story

Leads with data, builds to insight. Structure: "Here is what the data shows → Here is why it matters → Here is what we should do." Best for analytical audiences who trust data more than narrative. All claims are backed by visible data points. Example: "Our analysis of 18 months of transaction data shows..."

Problem-Solution Story

Opens with a pain point, diagnoses the root cause, proposes a solution. Structure: "Here is the problem → Here is what's causing it → Here is the solution → Here is the projected impact." Best for leadership presentations where you need a decision or budget approval. The data supports the argument rather than leading it.

Journey / Timeline Story

Follows events chronologically, building to a present-day situation and future projection. Structure: "Here is where we started → Here is what happened → Here is where we are today → Here is where we are headed." Best for annual reviews, post-mortems, and product launch recaps. Uses line charts and annotated timelines prominently.

Example: E-Commerce Story Arc

Story Title: "Why Revenue Grew 22% But Profit Fell 8% — A 5-Point Analysis"
  1. Story Point 1 — Traffic: Line chart showing website sessions grew 35% YoY. Caption: "Record traffic driven by paid social campaigns."
  2. Story Point 2 — Conversion: Funnel chart showing conversion rate declined from 3.2% → 2.4%. Caption: "Higher traffic didn't convert — checkout abandonment up 40%."
  3. Story Point 3 — Revenue: Revenue grew 22% (traffic volume overcame lower conversion). Bar chart by category showing Electronics driving growth. Caption: "Revenue grew despite poor conversion — Electronics saved the quarter."
  4. Story Point 4 — Retention: Cohort analysis showing repeat purchase rate fell from 42% → 28%. Caption: "New customers aren't returning — LTV is declining."
  5. Story Point 5 — Recommendations: Fix checkout UX (reduces abandonment), launch loyalty programme (improves retention), reduce paid social CAC. Caption: "Three actions to recapture ₹2.4Cr in lost margin."

✍ Summary & Exam Tips

Core Concepts to Know

  • Right chart for the right data: Time series → Line, Comparison → Bar, Distribution → Box plot/Histogram, Correlation → Scatter, Part-to-whole → Pie (≤5) / Stacked bar / Treemap
  • Tufte's data-ink ratio: Every pixel of ink in a chart should represent data. Eliminate gridlines, borders, 3D effects, and decorative fills that add no information.
  • Dimensions = Blue pills = Categorical = Creates headers. Measures = Green pills = Numeric = Creates axes.
  • Discrete vs Continuous: Discrete (blue) creates labels/headers. Continuous (green) creates axes.
  • Tableau product hierarchy: Prep (clean) → Desktop (author) → Server/Online (share) → Mobile/Reader (consume).
  • Dashboard design order: Purpose → Questions → KPIs → Sketch → Build → Interactivity → Test → Deploy.
  • Storyboard = narrative sequence: Context → Problem → Evidence → Recommendation.
  • Dashboard best practices: F-pattern layout, above-the-fold KPIs, 3-click rule, filter actions for interactivity.

Common Exam Traps

TrapCorrect Answer
Tableau Server vs Tableau Online Server = on-premise (you manage hardware). Online = cloud SaaS (Salesforce manages). Both have same governance features.
Bar chart for time series Use line chart for continuous time trends. Use bar chart only for discrete period comparisons (e.g., 3 annual totals).
Pie chart with 8+ segments Use stacked bar or treemap for part-to-whole with many categories. Pie chart is limited to 5 segments maximum.
Truncating y-axis on bar chart Bar charts must start at zero — bar length encodes value. Line charts may use non-zero baseline, but must label clearly.
Confusing Dimension and Measure defaults Tableau classifies as Measure if numeric, Dimension if text/date. You can change this classification (e.g., Postal Code is numeric but should be a Dimension).
Tableau Public privacy Tableau Public = zero privacy. Any data published is visible to the entire internet. Never use for confidential business data.
Choropleth for absolute values Choropleth uses colour fill → use for rates/ratios (per capita, %). Use symbol map (proportional circles) for absolute values to avoid geographic size bias.

Key Formulas to Memorise

Data-Ink Ratio = (Data Ink) / (Total Ink in the Graphic) → Maximise, target ≈ 1.0
Profit Margin % = (Revenue − Cost) / Revenue × 100
IQR = Q3 − Q1 | Outlier if value < Q1 − 1.5×IQR OR value > Q3 + 1.5×IQR
Tableau LOD: {FIXED [Dimension] : AGGR([Measure])} — e.g., {FIXED [Region] : SUM([Sales])}

NP5Revenue grew from ₹500,000 to ₹864,000 over 3 years. What is the Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR, %)?
CAGR = (End / Start)^(1/n) − 1 = (864,000 / 500,000)^(1/3) − 1 = (1.728)^(0.3333) − 1 = 1.20 − 1 = 0.20 = 20%

Q1 · 2 marks Regional sales are West = 156, East = 204, North = 120, South = 120 (₹ thousand). In a "% of Total" calculation, what share of total sales does the West region hold?
Divide the region's value by the grand total.
Total = 156 + 204 + 120 + 120 = 600 West % = 156 / 600 × 100 = 26.0%
The answer is 26.0%. (31% wrongly divides by East; 13% divides by an incorrect total of 1200.)
Q2 · 2 marks Sales were ₹4,00,000 in 2023 and ₹5,20,000 in 2024. What is the Year-over-Year (YoY) growth?
YoY growth divides the change by the earlier year.
YoY = (520000 − 400000) / 400000 × 100 = 120000 / 400000 × 100 = 30%
The answer is 30%. (23.1% wrongly divides by the later year; 120% forgets to subtract the base.)
Q3 · 2 marks A product's revenue grows from ₹1,00,000 to ₹1,61,051 over 5 years. What is its CAGR?
CAGR is the geometric (compound) annual rate.
CAGR = (End / Start)^(1/n) − 1 = (161051 / 100000)^(1/5) − 1 = 1.61051^0.2 − 1 = 1.10 − 1 = 10%
The answer is 10%. (12.2% wrongly divides total growth by 5; 61.1% is the cumulative, not annualised, growth.)
Q4 · 2 marks You need to show how a single measure (monthly sales) changes over time across a continuous date axis. According to chart-selection logic, which chart is most appropriate?
A continuous measure plotted against time calls for a line chart, which makes trend and direction immediately readable. A pie chart shows parts-of-a-whole (not time), a scatter plot shows the relationship between two measures, and a tree map shows hierarchical proportions.
Q5 · 2 marks In Tableau, a field is classified as a dimension rather than a measure when it primarily:
Dimensions are qualitative fields (Region, Category, Date) that partition the data; measures are quantitative fields that get aggregated. The answer is (A). Option (B) describes a measure; shelf placement (C, D) is flexible and does not define the field type.
Q6 · 2 marks Pre-attentive attributes such as colour, size and position are valued in visualisation design because they:
Pre-attentive attributes are perceived in milliseconds, before focused attention, so they let a designer direct the eye to what matters fastest. The answer is (C). The other options confuse perception with printing, file size, or statistics.
