1. Share of Wallet (SOW)

Definition & Formula

Share of Wallet measures what percentage of a customer's total spend within a category is captured by your company. Unlike market share (which looks at the whole market), SOW zooms in on the individual customer.

SOW (%) = (Customer Spend with Company / Customer's Total Category Spend) × 100
Worked Example — Coffee Chain:
A customer spends ₹500/month total across all coffee shops.
They spend ₹200/month at our coffee chain.

SOW = (200 / 500) × 100 = 40%

Interpretation: We capture 40 paise of every rupee this customer spends on coffee. The remaining 60% goes to competitors — a growth opportunity.

SOW vs Market Share

DimensionShare of WalletMarket Share
Unit of AnalysisIndividual customerEntire market / segment
Data SourceCustomer transaction data, surveysIndustry sales data, Nielsen reports
Use CaseRetention, upsell, cross-sellCompetitive positioning, strategic planning
ActionabilityHighly actionable — personalized campaignsStrategic & aggregate
Typical Metric% of customer's category spend% of total industry revenue

Key Insight: A company can have a high market share but low SOW per customer — meaning many customers buy from them but also frequently buy from competitors.

Amazon SOW Strategy — Case Study

Amazon is the world's foremost example of SOW maximisation through ecosystem lock-in:

  • Prime Membership: Annual fee creates switching costs — customers justify subscription by buying more on Amazon
  • Recommendations Engine: "Customers also bought" cross-sells across categories (electronics → accessories → consumables)
  • Product Breadth: 350M+ products — one-stop-shop reduces motivation to visit competitors
  • Amazon Fresh / Whole Foods: Captures grocery SOW (previously at supermarkets)
  • Fire TV / Echo Devices: Device ecosystem creates content SOW and habitual Amazon usage
  • AWS / Alexa Skills: B2B and developer ecosystem extends SOW into enterprise
Result: Prime members spend on average 4× more annually than non-Prime members. That is SOW strategy working at scale.

Strategies to Increase Share of Wallet

Growth Levers

  1. Loyalty Programs: Reward incremental purchases to increase stickiness (Starbucks Stars, airline miles)
  2. Cross-Selling: Recommend adjacent categories the customer currently buys elsewhere
  3. Upselling: Higher-tier versions of existing purchases (economy → business class)
  4. Subscription / Auto-replenishment: Locks in repeat purchases (Amazon Subscribe & Save)
  5. Bundle Deals: Price incentive to buy multiple categories at once

2. Market Basket Analysis (Association Mining)

Purpose & Business Applications

Market Basket Analysis (MBA) discovers which products are frequently purchased together. It enables:

  • Retail store layout: Place frequently associated products near each other
  • E-commerce recommendations: "Frequently bought together" widgets
  • Bundle pricing: Offer discounts on associated product pairs
  • Inventory planning: Stock complementary items together
  • Menu design: In restaurants, identify natural meal combinations

Key Metrics & Formulas

Support(A) = Transactions containing A / Total Transactions
Measures: How frequently does item A appear overall?
Confidence(A → B) = Support(A ∪ B) / Support(A)
Measures: Given A is bought, how often is B also bought? (Rule reliability)
Lift(A → B) = Confidence(A → B) / Support(B)
Measures: How much more likely is B bought WITH A versus randomly?
Lift > 1: Positive association  |  Lift = 1: Independent  |  Lift < 1: Negative
Leverage(A → B) = Support(A ∪ B) − Support(A) × Support(B)
Measures: Deviation from independence. Positive = complementary products.
Conviction(A → B) = (1 − Support(B)) / (1 − Confidence(A → B))
Measures: Rule directionality. ∞ = perfect rule (B always follows A)

Dataset: 10 transactions, Items: Apple (A), Banana (B), Cherry (C), Dates (D)

Transactions:

T6:{A,C,D}   T7:{B}   T8:{A,B,C}   T9:{C,D}   T10:{A,B}

Rule Calculations:

Confidence(A → B) = Support(A∪B) / Support(A) = 0.50 / 0.70 = 0.714
Interpretation: 71.4% of Apple buyers also buy Banana.

Lift(A → B) = Confidence(A→B) / Support(B) = 0.714 / 0.70 = 1.02
Interpretation: Apple buyers are 1.02× more likely to buy Banana than a random customer. (Mild positive association)

Confidence(A → C) = 0.40 / 0.70 = 0.571
Lift(A → C) = 0.571 / 0.60 = 0.952
Interpretation: Lift < 1 — Apple buyers are slightly less likely to buy Cherry than average. Slight negative association.

Leverage(A → B) = 0.50 − (0.70 × 0.70) = 0.50 − 0.49 = +0.01 (mildly complementary)

3. RFM Analysis (Detailed)

The Three Dimensions

R — Recency

Days since last purchase. More recent = better. A customer who bought yesterday is more engaged than one who bought 18 months ago.

R Score: 4 = 1–90 days
3 = 91–180 days
2 = 181–365 days
1 = >365 days

F — Frequency

Number of transactions in a period. More = better. High frequency signals loyalty and habitual purchasing.

F Score: 4 = >12 orders/year
3 = 7–12 orders
2 = 3–6 orders
1 = 1–2 orders

M — Monetary

Total spend in the period. Higher = more valuable. Combine with F to identify high-value but infrequent buyers (big ticket items) vs. low-value frequent buyers.

M Score: 4 = >₹50,000  |  3 = ₹20,000–50,000  |  2 = ₹5,000–20,000  |  1 = <₹5,000

Real-World Applications

Amazon

  • Personalized homepage driven by F and M scores
  • Prime upgrade offers targeted at high-R, low-F customers
  • "It's been a while" emails triggered by R score drop
  • Re-engagement discount for "At Risk" customers

Fuel Company / B2B

  • High-M, high-F = fleet operators → fleet contract offer
  • High-R, low-F = casual drivers → loyalty card push
  • Low-R = lapsed fleets → account manager outreach

Python RFM Implementation

import pandas as pd from datetime import datetime # df columns: customer_id, order_date, order_id, order_value snapshot_date = datetime(2024, 12, 31) rfm = df.groupby('customer_id').agg( R=('order_date', lambda x: (snapshot_date - x.max()).days), F=('order_id', 'count'), M=('order_value', 'sum') ).reset_index() # Score 1-4 using quartiles rfm['R_score'] = pd.qcut(rfm['R'], q=4, labels=[4, 3, 2, 1]) # Lower days = better R rfm['F_score'] = pd.qcut(rfm['F'], q=4, labels=[1, 2, 3, 4]) # Higher orders = better F rfm['M_score'] = pd.qcut(rfm['M'], q=4, labels=[1, 2, 3, 4]) # Higher spend = better M rfm['RFM_Score'] = rfm['R_score'].astype(str) + rfm['F_score'].astype(str) + rfm['M_score'].astype(str) # Segment mapping def segment(row): r, f, m = int(row['R_score']), int(row['F_score']), int(row['M_score']) if r == 4 and f == 4 and m == 4: return 'Champion' if r >= 3 and f >= 3: return 'Loyal Customer' if r == 1 and f >= 4 and m >= 4: return "Can't Lose Them" if r == 1 and f == 1 and m == 1: return 'Lost' return 'Other' rfm['Segment'] = rfm.apply(segment, axis=1) print(rfm.head(10))

5. Summary & Exam Tips

Key Formulas to Memorise

SOW (%) = (Customer Spend with Us / Total Category Spend) × 100
Support(A) = Transactions with A / Total Transactions
Confidence(A → B) = Support(A ∪ B) / Support(A)
Lift(A → B) = Confidence(A → B) / Support(B)
Leverage(A → B) = Support(A ∪ B) − Support(A) × Support(B)

Exam Tips

  • RFM Recency trap: R score 4 means the customer bought most recently (low days). Do not reverse this. Fewer days = higher R score.
  • Lift interpretation: Lift = 1 is the baseline (independence). Memorise: >1 = buy together more than chance, <1 = less than chance.
  • SOW vs Market Share: Always state the unit of analysis — individual customer (SOW) vs. whole market (Market Share).
  • FP-Growth is fastest for large datasets; Apriori is slowest but conceptually simplest. Exam questions often ask "which is most efficient?"
  • 444 = Champion; 111 = Lost. For RFM 141/144 = "Can't Lose Them" — historically high value but recently disengaged. Requires urgent action.
  • Confidence is not symmetric: Confidence(A→B) ≠ Confidence(B→A) unless Support(A) = Support(B).
  • Amazon Fresh capturing grocery SOW is a frequently-cited exam example for category-level SOW expansion.

NP1A customer spends ₹60,000 per year across the coffee category, of which ₹18,000 goes to Brand X. What is Brand X's Share of Wallet (%)?
SOW = (Spending with Company / Total Category Spending) × 100 = (18,000 / 60,000) × 100 = 30%
30% of this customer's coffee budget goes to Brand X; the remaining 70% is up for grabs.
NP2In a transaction log of 600 orders, the itemset {Milk, Bread} appears together in 150 orders. What is the Support of {Milk, Bread} (as a decimal)?
Support = Transactions containing itemset / Total transactions = 150 / 600 = 0.25
The pair co-occurs in 25% of all baskets.
NP3Support(Bread ∪ Butter) = 0.18 and Support(Bread) = 0.45. What is the Confidence of the rule Bread → Butter?
Confidence(A → B) = Support(A ∪ B) / Support(A) = 0.18 / 0.45 = 0.40
Of all baskets containing bread, 40% also contain butter.
NP4For the rule A → B, Confidence(A → B) = 0.60 and Support(B) = 0.40. Compute the Lift.
Lift(A → B) = Confidence(A → B) / Support(B) = 0.60 / 0.40 = 1.5
Lift > 1 ⇒ A and B are positively correlated; buying A makes B 1.5× more likely than baseline.
NP5Support(A ∩ B) = 0.20, Support(A) = 0.40, Support(B) = 0.50. Compute the Lift using Lift = P(A∩B) / (P(A)·P(B)).
Lift = P(A ∩ B) / (P(A) × P(B)) = 0.20 / (0.40 × 0.50) = 0.20 / 0.20 = 1.0
Lift = 1 means A and B are statistically independent — no useful association.
NP6After a loyalty campaign, the same coffee customer (total category spend still ₹60,000) now directs ₹27,000 to Brand X. What is the new Share of Wallet (%)?
SOW = (27,000 / 60,000) × 100 = 45%
SOW rose from 30% to 45% — a 15-point gain in this customer's wallet share.
NP7Out of 800 transactions, {Chips, Soda} co-occur in 240 of them. Express the Support of {Chips, Soda} as a percentage.
Support = (240 / 800) × 100 = 30%
A 30% support is high — this pair would be a strong candidate for co-placement on the shelf.
NP8For items A and B: P(A∩B) = 0.30, P(A) = 0.50, P(B) = 0.40. Compute the Leverage = P(A∩B) − P(A)·P(B).
Leverage = P(A ∩ B) − P(A) × P(B) = 0.30 − (0.50 × 0.40) = 0.30 − 0.20 = 0.10
Leverage > 0 ⇒ the pair co-occurs more often than chance would predict — a genuine association.
NP9Of 500 customers who bought a laptop, 145 also bought a carry bag. What is the cross-sell rate (%) for the bag?
Cross-sell % = Buyers of both / Buyers of primary × 100 = 145 / 500 × 100 = 29%
This is also the confidence of the rule Laptop → Bag (0.29).
NP10In a 1–4 quartile RFM system (4 = best), a customer scores R=4, F=4, M=4 (very recent, very frequent, top spender). Which segment do they belong to?
High recency, frequency and monetary scores (4-4-4) define Champions — the most valuable segment, to be rewarded with special offers. Hibernating/At-Risk customers have low recency and frequency.

Q1 · 2 marks A customer spends ₹1,00,000 per year across the fitness-apparel category, of which ₹35,000 goes to Brand Z. What is Brand Z's Share of Wallet?
SOW = (Spend with Company / Total Category Spend) × 100 = (35,000 / 1,00,000) × 100 = 35%
Brand Z captures 35 paise of every rupee this customer spends on fitness apparel; the other 65% is competitor share — the growth headroom.
Q2 · 2 marks For the rule {Coffee} → {Cake}, Support(Coffee ∪ Cake) = 0.21 and Support(Coffee) = 0.35. What is the Confidence of the rule?
Confidence(A → B) = Support(A ∪ B) / Support(A) = 0.21 / 0.35 = 0.60
Of all baskets containing coffee, 60% also contain cake. (1.67 inverts the ratio; 0.075 multiplies instead of divides.)
Q3 · 2 marks For the rule {Diapers} → {Beer}, Confidence = 0.72 and the overall Support(Beer) = 0.45. Compute the Lift.
Lift(A → B) = Confidence(A → B) / Support(B) = 0.72 / 0.45 = 1.6
Lift = 1.6 > 1 ⇒ buyers of diapers are 1.6× more likely than the average shopper to also buy beer — a positive, actionable association. (0.625 inverts the ratio.)
Q4 · 2 marks A market-basket analysis finds that the rule {Bread} → {Jam} has a Lift of 0.7. How should a category manager interpret and act on this?
Lift < 1 ⇒ negative correlation · Lift = 1 ⇒ independent · Lift > 1 ⇒ positive
A Lift of 0.7 (< 1) means bread buyers are less likely than average to buy jam — bundling or co-location would not pay off. Lift = 1 (not 0.7) would mean independence.
Q5 · 2 marks In market-basket analysis, what is the key distinction between Support and Confidence for a rule A → B?
Support(A∪B) = P(A and B) — frequency across ALL baskets Confidence(A→B) = P(B | A) = Support(A∪B) / Support(A)
Support tells you how often the combination occurs at all (popularity); Confidence tells you how reliable the "if A then B" prediction is. A rule needs both high support (worth acting on) and high confidence (trustworthy).
Q6 · 2 marks An RFM analysis scores a customer 1-1-5 (Recency = 1, Frequency = 1, Monetary = 5 on a 1–5 best scale). Which segment label best fits, and what action is most appropriate?
R=1 (long since last purchase) + F=1 (rarely buys) + M=5 (spends big when they do)
A high-monetary customer who has lapsed (low recency) is a Can't-Lose / At-Risk high-value account — the priority is targeted win-back, because their spend is large. Champions score high on all three; a low-monetary lapsed buyer would be "Lost".
