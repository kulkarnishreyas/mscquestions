1. Integrated Marketing Communications (IMC)

Definition & Core Concept

Integrated Marketing Communications (IMC) is the strategic coordination of all promotional tools and communication channels so that they deliver a single, consistent message to the target audience across every touchpoint. Rather than running advertising, PR, social media, and events in siloes, IMC ensures that every customer interaction reinforces the same brand promise.

Key Principle: The whole is greater than the sum of its parts — synergy across channels amplifies reach, recall, and response rates beyond what any single channel can achieve alone.

IMC was popularised by Don Schultz (Northwestern University) in the early 1990s and is now the industry standard for strategic brand communication planning.

2. Media Planning & Metrics

The Media Planning Process

Media planning is the systematic process of deciding where, when, and how often to place advertisements to achieve marketing objectives most efficiently.

6-Step Media Planning Process:
  1. Set Objectives — Define reach targets, frequency goals, campaign duration
  2. Define Target Audience — Demographics, psychographics, media consumption habits
  3. Channel Selection — Choose media mix (TV, digital, print, OOH, radio)
  4. Budget Allocation — Distribute spending across channels using chosen method
  5. Scheduling — Plan timing: continuity, flighting, or pulsing
  6. Measurement & Optimisation — Track KPIs, reallocate budget to best performers

Core Media Metrics & Formulas

GRP — Gross Rating Points

GRP = Reach (%) × Frequency
Example: A campaign reaches 40% of the target audience with an average frequency of 3 exposures.
GRP = 40 × 3 = 120 GRPs

Interpretation: GRP of 120 means you delivered 120 "rating point equivalents" of exposure weight. Higher GRP = greater overall campaign delivery, but must be balanced — extremely high frequency without broad reach wastes budget on already-saturated viewers.

CPM — Cost Per Thousand Impressions

CPM = (Total Cost / Total Impressions) × 1,000
Example: Ad campaign costs ₹50,000 and generates 5,00,000 impressions.
CPM = (50,000 / 5,00,000) × 1,000 = ₹100 per thousand impressions

Use case: CPM is the standard metric for comparing efficiency across media buys for awareness campaigns where impression volume matters.

CPC — Cost Per Click

CPC = Total Cost / Total Clicks
Example: Digital campaign costs ₹10,000 and generates 500 clicks.
CPC = 10,000 / 500 = ₹20 per click

Use case: CPC is used for performance/direct-response campaigns where driving traffic or conversions is the goal (Google Ads, Facebook Ads).

SOV — Share of Voice

SOV (%) = (Brand Ad Spend / Total Category Ad Spend) × 100
Example: Brand spends ₹20 lakh; total category (all brands) spends ₹1 crore.
SOV = (20,00,000 / 1,00,00,000) × 100 = 20%

Strategic insight: If SOV > SOM (Share of Market), the brand is investing aggressively to grow. If SOV < SOM, it may be coasting on existing equity (SOV–SOM gap theory by Binet & Field).

3. Adstock Model

What is Adstock?

The Adstock model, introduced by Simon Broadbent in 1979, captures the fact that advertising effects do not vanish immediately when spending stops. Instead, they carry over and decay gradually over time — consumers remember ads they saw last week, last month, or even longer ago.

This "memory effect" means that current advertising effectiveness is a combination of current spend plus the residual effect of past spend. Ignoring adstock leads to underestimating advertising ROI for campaigns that ran weeks ago.

Key Insight: A brand that stops advertising does not instantly lose its advertising effect. The effect decays geometrically — this is why "dark periods" in flighting schedules can still maintain some brand presence.

Saturation & Diminishing Returns: As advertising spend continues, its incremental impact decreases — the classic law of diminishing returns. The Adstock model also accounts for the saturation point beyond which additional advertising yields only minimal additional return, helping planners avoid over-spending.

Adstock Formula

At = α × At−1 + It

Where:

  • At = Adstock (effective advertising level) at time t
  • α = Decay factor (retention rate), between 0 and 1
  • At−1 = Adstock from the previous period
  • It = New advertising spend/GRPs at time t

Decay Factor α — Interpretation

α ValueMemory SpeedHalf-LifeTypical Use Case
α = 0.9Very slow decay~6.6 weeksBrand-building campaigns, luxury goods
α = 0.7Moderate decay~2.0 weeksFMCG, mid-term campaigns
α = 0.5Faster decay1.0 weekPromotional campaigns, tactical activity
α = 0.3Fast decay~0.4 weeksVery short-term activation
α = 0.0No memoryInstantPure week-by-week spend (no carryover)
Half-Life = log(0.5) / log(α)   (in periods)
Example: If α = 0.5:
Half-Life = log(0.5) / log(0.5) = 1.0 week (adstock halves each week)

If α = 0.7:
Half-Life = log(0.5) / log(0.7) = −0.301 / −0.155 ≈ 1.94 weeks

Worked Example: α = 0.5, Spend = [100, 0, 0, 50, 0, 0]

4. VATS Model & Marketing Mix Modelling (MMM)

What is Marketing Mix Modelling?

Marketing Mix Modelling (MMM) is a statistical technique (usually ordinary least squares or Bayesian regression) that decomposes total sales into contributions from each marketing and non-marketing driver. It answers the question: "Of all the sales we made this quarter, how much came from TV, how much from digital, how much from seasonality, and how much would have happened anyway?"

  • Separates organic (baseline) sales from marketing-driven incremental sales
  • Quantifies the ROI of each individual media channel
  • Enables optimal budget reallocation across channels
  • Accounts for external factors (seasonality, economic conditions, competitor activity)
Why MMM over last-click attribution? MMM uses aggregate time-series data and can capture offline channels (TV, radio, print) that digital attribution cannot. It is privacy-safe (no cookies/user IDs needed) and increasingly relevant in a post-cookie world.

The VATS Model Formula

Yt = β0 + Σ(βi × Xi,t) + εt

Where:

  • Yt = Total sales (or revenue) at time period t
  • β0 = Baseline sales — what you would sell with zero marketing activity (driven by organic demand, distribution, price, brand equity)
  • βi = Media/channel coefficient — the incremental sales generated per unit of spend/GRP in channel i
  • Xi,t = Spend or GRPs for channel i at time t (often transformed with adstock first)
  • εt = Error term — unexplained variation not captured by the model

Expanded VATS Specification

Yt = β0 + βTV×AdstockTVt + βDig×AdstockDigt + βPromo×Promot + βSeas×Seasont + εt
Interpretation Example:
β0 = 50,000 units (baseline — organic demand)
βTV = 2.5 units per GRP (TV coefficient)
AdstockTVt = 80 GRPs (current adstock-adjusted TV exposure)
Contribution from TV = 2.5 × 80 = 200 units

So TV is responsible for 200 units of sales this week on top of the 50,000 baseline.

5. Promotion Effectiveness

Why Measure Promotion Effectiveness?

Sales promotions (discounts, BOGOs, coupons, in-store offers) are expensive. Brands must distinguish between:

  • Genuine incremental sales — new buyers, increased consumption
  • Pull-forward effect — loyal buyers stocking up at discounted price (no new revenue)
  • Cannibalisation — promo product steals sales from other products in the same portfolio
Rule of Thumb: A promotion that generates only pull-forward or cannibalisation with no true incrementality is destroying margin, not building the business.

Core Promotion Metrics

Sales Uplift

Sales Uplift (%) = [(Salesduring − Salesbaseline) / Salesbaseline] × 100%

Promotional ROI

Promo ROI (%) = [(Incremental Revenue − Promo Cost) / Promo Cost] × 100%

Where: Incremental Revenue = Salesduring − Salesbaseline

Cannibalisation Rate

Cannibalisation Rate (%) = (Loss in Other Products / Gain in Promoted Product) × 100%
Worked Example — Full Promo Analysis:

Given:
• Baseline weekly sales (before promo): ₹1,00,000
• Sales during promotion week: ₹1,40,000
• Promotion cost (discount + execution): ₹20,000

Step 1 — Sales Uplift:
Uplift = [(1,40,000 − 1,00,000) / 1,00,000] × 100 = (40,000 / 1,00,000) × 100 = 40%

Step 2 — Incremental Revenue:
Incremental Revenue = ₹1,40,000 − ₹1,00,000 = ₹40,000

Step 3 — Promo ROI:
ROI = [(40,000 − 20,000) / 20,000] × 100 = [20,000 / 20,000] × 100 = 100%

Interpretation: For every ₹1 spent on the promotion, ₹1 of incremental profit was generated (after promo cost). A 100% Promo ROI means the promotion doubled the money invested in it.

Cannibalisation vs Halo Effect

Cannibalisation (Negative Spillover)

When promoting Product A causes lost sales of Product B in the same portfolio — consumers switch rather than incrementally buy more.

Example: Promoting Lays Original causes ₹5,000 drop in Lays Masala sales while Lays Original gained ₹20,000.
Cannibalisation Rate = 5,000 / 20,000 × 100 = 25%

Strategies to minimise: Target non-buyers, use targeted coupons, avoid deep discounts on category staples.

Halo Effect (Positive Spillover)

When promoting Product A causes increased sales of related products — consumers drawn to the store/brand buy other items too.

Example: Promoting Maggi noodles drives a 12% uplift in Maggi ketchup and sauces — the halo from brand activation extends across the portfolio.

Maximise halo by: Cross-merchandising, bundle displays, category management placement near promoted SKU.

Post-Promotion Dip

A classic pattern where sales fall below baseline in the period immediately following a promotion. This occurs because:

  • Consumers stocked up during the promotion (pull-forward)
  • Pantry inventory delays their next purchase
  • Deal-seekers return to waiting for the next promotion
True Incrementality Check: Measure the net sales uplift over the full promotion + recovery window. If post-promo dip erases the uplift, the promotion had zero true incrementality and destroyed margin.

6. Return on Marketing Investment (ROMI)

What is ROMI?

Return on Marketing Investment (ROMI) is the most comprehensive metric for evaluating marketing financial performance. It measures how much incremental profit is generated for every rupee (or dollar) invested in marketing.

Unlike generic ROI, ROMI is marketing-specific: it accounts for attribution (what revenue is genuinely caused by marketing) and gross margin (not all revenue is profit).

ROMI Formulas

Basic ROMI (Revenue-based)

ROMI (%) = [(Revenue from Marketing − Marketing Cost) / Marketing Cost] × 100%

Margin-Adjusted ROMI (Correct Method)

ROMI (%) = [(Incremental Revenue × Gross Margin% − Marketing Cost) / Marketing Cost] × 100%
Why use margin-adjusted ROMI? Revenue is not the same as profit. If your gross margin is 40%, then ₹1,00,000 of revenue only generates ₹40,000 of gross profit. Comparing ₹1,00,000 revenue to ₹75,000 marketing cost inflates the apparent return. Margin-adjusted ROMI gives the true picture.
Worked Example — Margin-Adjusted ROMI:

Given:
• Attributable Revenue (incremental, from marketing): ₹3,00,000
• Marketing Cost: ₹75,000
• Gross Margin: 40%

Step 1 — Gross Profit from Marketing:
Gross Profit = ₹3,00,000 × 0.40 = ₹1,20,000

Step 2 — Net Incremental Profit:
Net Profit = ₹1,20,000 − ₹75,000 = ₹45,000

Step 3 — ROMI:
ROMI = (₹45,000 / ₹75,000) × 100 = 60%

Interpretation: For every ₹1 invested in marketing, ₹0.60 of gross profit was generated above and beyond the marketing cost. A ROMI of 60% is in the "Good" range.

ROMI Benchmark Framework

Conceptual Summaries

IMC & Media Planning

  • IMC = one consistent message across ALL channels simultaneously
  • Coca-Cola & Nike: consistency + emotional resonance = brand equity compounding
  • GRP = total weight of delivery (reach × frequency)
  • CPM = awareness efficiency; CPC = performance efficiency
  • Scheduling: Continuity = steady; Flighting = bursts + dark; Pulsing = base + peaks
  • SOV > SOM → brand is aggressively investing for growth

Adstock & MMM

  • Adstock captures carryover: effects persist even when spending stops
  • Higher α = longer memory = effect decays more slowly
  • α = 0.5 → half-life = 1 week; α = 0.9 → half-life ≈ 6.6 weeks
  • MMM decomposes sales into baseline + each media driver
  • VATS β₀ = baseline organic demand (no marketing)
  • Validate MMM with R² > 0.85 and MAPE < 10%

Promotion Effectiveness

  • Uplift = (Salespromo − Baseline) / Baseline × 100
  • Promo ROI = 100% means incremental revenue = 2× promo cost
  • Cannibalisation: promo product steals from portfolio siblings
  • Halo: promo lifts related products (positive spillover)
  • Watch for post-promo dip — can erase true incrementality

ROMI

  • Always use gross margin in ROMI — revenue ≠ profit
  • >100% = excellent; 50–100% = good; 0–50% = marginal; <0% = loss
  • ROMI uses attributed revenue; generic ROI does not require attribution
  • Digital Search typically has highest ROMI; TV/print lowest
  • Use MMM outputs to calculate channel-level ROMI for budget decisions

Common Exam Traps

  • ROMI without margin: Many students calculate ROMI using raw revenue, not gross profit. Always multiply incremental revenue by gross margin % first.
  • CPM vs CPC confusion: CPM is per 1,000 impressions (awareness); CPC is per click (engagement). They measure fundamentally different things.
  • Flighting vs Pulsing: Flighting has complete dark periods (zero spend); Pulsing maintains a continuous base level with higher peaks. They are NOT the same.
  • Adstock α = 0 vs α = 1: α = 0 means no carryover (pure current spend); α = 1 means infinite memory (spend accumulates forever). Real-world values are between 0.3 and 0.9.
  • Cannibalisation rate formula: It is Loss in other products divided by Gain in the promoted product — not the other way round.
  • GRP > 100 is possible: GRP can exceed 100 because it is Reach% × Frequency. A campaign with 50% reach and frequency of 4 gives GRP = 200 (some people seen it 4 times).

Q1 · 2 marks A campaign costs ₹1,20,000 and generates ₹4,80,000 in incremental revenue attributable to the campaign. What is its ROMI?

GRP = Reach × Frequency = 70 × 3.5 = 245
The answer is 245 GRPs. (20 wrongly divides reach by frequency — that gives reach, not GRP.)

CPP is the cost of one rating point.
CPP = Cost / GRP = 84,000 / 240 = ₹350
The answer is ₹350 per rating point. (₹285.7 inverts the division; multiplying gives the absurd ₹20,16,000.)
Q4 · 2 marks Using the adstock model Aₜ = Iₜ + λ·Aₜ₋₁ with a decay rate λ = 0.6, this week's media impact Iₜ = 500 and last week's adstock Aₜ₋₁ = 400. What is this week's adstock Aₜ?
Add the current impact to the decayed carry-over of last period.
Aₜ = Iₜ + λ · Aₜ₋₁ = 500 + 0.6 × 400 = 500 + 240 = 740
The answer is 740. (900 forgets to apply λ; 540 mistakenly applies λ to the current impact.)
Q5 · 2 marks The "carry-over" (lagged) effect captured by the adstock model reflects which advertising reality?
Adstock encodes that exposure builds memory that persists but decays at rate λ each period — the carry-over effect. The answer is (B). Option (D) describes growth rather than decay; (C) describes a constant linear (non-saturating) response, which adstock does not assume.
Q6 · 2 marks The core principle of Integrated Marketing Communications (IMC) is best described as:
IMC is about consistency and coordination across all touchpoints so the audience receives a single, clear, reinforcing brand message. The answer is (D). Option (B) is the opposite of integration; (A) and (C) are budgeting/channel choices, not the IMC principle.
