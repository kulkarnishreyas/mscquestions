1. Customer Centricity — Evolution of Marketing Philosophy

The relationship between businesses and customers has evolved dramatically over the past century. Understanding this evolution helps explain why customer analytics matters so much today.

Era 1: Product-Centric
Pre-1950s

"Build it and they will come." Supply was scarce; customers had little choice. Henry Ford famously said: "Any customer can have a car painted any color, so long as it is black." Efficiency over customisation.

  • Mass production focus
  • Standardisation for efficiency
  • Seller's market — demand > supply
Era 2: Marketing-Centric
1950s–1980s

Post-war prosperity, supply exceeding demand. Advertising emerges as a force. "Mad Men" era — push marketing, brand slogans, TV commercials. The 4Ps (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) codified.

  • Advertising-driven growth
  • Broadcast / push marketing
  • Market segmentation begins
Era 3: Customer-Focused
1980s–2000s

CRM systems emerge (Siebel, early Salesforce). Metrics like CSAT introduced. Customer complaints tracked. TQM and service quality movements. But still largely reactive — fixing problems rather than predicting needs.

  • CRM adoption, call centres
  • CSAT / service quality metrics
  • Loyalty programmes emerge
Era 4: Customer-Centric
2000s–Present

Data-driven personalisation. Individual customer lifetime value, predictive analytics, AI-driven recommendations. Amazon "customers also bought", Netflix personalisation. Company strategy built around customer value creation.

  • Personalisation at scale
  • CLV-driven decisions
  • Omnichannel, real-time data

2. Customer Journey Mapping

A customer journey map is a visual representation of every interaction a customer has with your brand — capturing not just actions, but emotions, pain points, and opportunities at each stage.

The 6 Components of a Journey Map

#ComponentDescriptionExample
1StagesThe phases of the customer's relationship with the brandAwareness → Consideration → Purchase → Retention → Advocacy
2TouchpointsEvery interaction channel between customer and brandWebsite, app, store, email, WhatsApp, call centre, social media
3Customer ActionsWhat the customer actually does at each touchpointSearches on Google, reads reviews, adds to cart, contacts support
4EmotionsHow the customer feels during each interactionExcited (discovery), frustrated (long checkout), relieved (issue resolved)
5Pain PointsFriction, obstacles, and problems in the experienceConfusing pricing, slow delivery updates, difficult returns process
6OpportunitiesActions the brand can take to improve each stageSimplify checkout, add real-time tracking, create self-service returns portal

4 Types of Customer Journey Maps

Current State Map

Documents the existing customer experience as it actually is today — warts and all. Used to identify problems and baseline for improvement.

Future State Map

Depicts the ideal future experience after improvements. Used for visioning, alignment, and prioritising investment in CX improvements.

Day-in-the-Life Map

Zooms out beyond brand interactions — shows the customer's full day. Reveals context: when they use the product, competing priorities, mood at different moments.

Service Blueprint

Adds internal operational layer — what front-stage customer sees AND back-stage processes enabling it. Identifies operational root causes of CX issues.

3. CX Metrics — NPS, CSAT, and CES

Net Promoter Score (NPS)

Question: "On a scale of 0–10, how likely are you to recommend us to a friend or colleague?"

ScoreCategoryHow they act
9–10PromotersActively recommend; loyal; grow LTV
7–8PassivesSatisfied but neutral; open to switching
0–6DetractorsUnhappy; risk negative word-of-mouth
NPS = %Promoters − %Detractors
(Range: −100 to +100)
Example: 100 respondents
40 gave 9–10 (Promoters = 40%)
30 gave 7–8 (Passives = 30%)
30 gave 0–6 (Detractors = 30%)
NPS = 40 − 30 = +10

Benchmarks: World-class >70 | Good 50–70 | Average 30–50 | Needs work <30

CSAT — Customer Satisfaction Score

Question: "How satisfied were you with this interaction? 1 (Very Dissatisfied) – 5 (Very Satisfied)"

Scores of 4 or 5 are counted as "satisfied".

CSAT = (Satisfied Responses / Total Responses) × 100
Satisfied = those scoring 4 or 5 out of 5
Example: Post-support survey
400 customers responded
320 rated 4 or 5 out of 5
CSAT = (320/400) × 100 = 80%

Industry benchmarks: Telecom ~70% | Banking ~78% | Retail ~82%
Target: 75–85% for most industries

Best used for: Specific interaction quality (support call, checkout experience)

CES — Customer Effort Score

Question: "How easy was it to resolve your issue today? 1 (Very Difficult) – 7 (Very Easy)"

Lower effort = better CX (paradoxically, a high score on the 1–7 scale = easy = good)

CES = Average score on 1–7 scale
High CES (near 7) = low effort = good
Low CES (near 1) = high effort = bad
Example: After customer service interaction
Average CES score: 5.5 / 7
→ Moderate effort required
Needs improvement — aim for 6.0+

Research: High-effort experiences are 4× more likely to lead to churn than low-effort ones. (CEB/Gartner research)

Best used for: Detecting friction in service / support interactions

4. Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)

CLV Formula

CLV = AOV × Purchase Frequency × Customer Lifespan × Profit Margin

Where:
AOV (Avg Order Value) = Total Revenue / Number of Orders
Purchase Frequency = Number of Orders / Number of Unique Customers
Customer Lifespan = 1 / Churn Rate (in same time unit as frequency)
Profit Margin = Net Profit / Revenue

Worked Example — E-commerce Brand

Given data:
Average Order Value (AOV) = ₹2,500
Purchase Frequency = 4 orders/year
Average Customer Lifespan = 3 years (annual churn rate = 1/3 = 33.3%)
Net Profit Margin = 25%

CLV Calculation:
CLV = ₹2,500 × 4 × 3 × 0.25 = ₹7,500

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) = ₹1,500
CLV : CAC = 7,500 : 1,500 = 5 : 1 ✓ Healthy!

CLV : CAC Ratio — Benchmarks

CLV : CAC Ratio = Customer Lifetime Value / Customer Acquisition Cost

Benchmark 3:1 → For every ₹1 spent acquiring a customer, earn ₹3 over their lifetime
< 1:1 → Losing money on every customer (unsustainable)
1:1 – 3:1 → Barely viable; reduce CAC or improve retention
3:1 – 5:1 → Healthy business model
> 5:1 → May be underinvesting in acquisition (leaving growth on the table)

CLV by Customer Segment — RFM-Based Strategy

SegmentCLV RangeCharacteristicsStrategy
Champions₹20,000+High frequency, recent, high valueVIP treatment, exclusive offers, brand ambassadors
Potential Loyalists₹8,000–20,000Growing engagement, regular buyersUpsell, loyalty programme, personal recommendations

Exam Tip — CLV Lifespan & Churn Relationship: If annual churn rate = 25%, then average lifespan = 1/0.25 = 4 years. If monthly churn = 5%, monthly lifespan = 1/0.05 = 20 months. Always match the churn rate time unit to the frequency time unit.

Why BERT beats lexicon-based methods: BERT is context-aware. "The movie was not good" — a simple lexicon scores "good" as positive and outputs +1. BERT reads the sentence holistically and correctly identifies "not good" as negative. Similarly, sarcasm ("Oh great, another delay!") is handled much better by transformer models.

Marketing Applications of Sentiment Analysis

  • Brand monitoring: Track brand sentiment across social media in real time (Twitter, Reddit, news)
  • Product review analysis: Auto-categorise thousands of reviews by product feature (battery, camera, packaging)
  • Social listening: Detect PR crises early — unusual spike in negative sentiment triggers alert
  • Customer feedback routing: Automatically route angry feedback to priority support queue
  • Competitor analysis: Compare your brand sentiment vs. competitors

6. Customer Churn Analysis

Churn Formulas

Customer Churn Rate = (Customers Lost in Period / Customers at Start of Period) × 100

Revenue Churn Rate = (MRR Lost in Period / MRR at Start of Period) × 100

Note: Revenue churn can be NEGATIVE (net expansion) if upsells exceed losses

Types of Churn

TypeDefinitionCauseHow to Address
Voluntary ChurnCustomer actively decides to cancelBetter competitor, price dissatisfaction, unmet needsCompetitive pricing, proactive re-engagement, feature improvement
Involuntary ChurnSubscription lapses without customer intentFailed payment, expired card, technical errorDunning management, payment retry logic, proactive reminders
Revenue ChurnRevenue lost even if customer stays (downgrade)Customer moves from Premium to Free tierValue-based upgrade nudges, usage-based pricing
Delinquent ChurnPayment fails repeatedly; account deactivatedCredit card expired or declinedSmart payment retries (avoid weekend/end-of-month retries)

8. Summary & Exam Tips

Key Formulas — Revision Card

1. NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors [range: −100 to +100]
2. CSAT = (Satisfied responses / Total responses) × 100
3. CLV = AOV × Purchase Frequency × Customer Lifespan × Profit Margin
4. Customer Lifespan = 1 / Annual Churn Rate
5. CLV:CAC Ratio = CLV / Customer Acquisition Cost [benchmark: 3:1]
6. Churn Rate = (Customers Lost / Customers at Start) × 100
7. Revenue Churn = (MRR Lost / MRR at Start) × 100
8. Sentiment Polarity: −1 (very negative) → 0 (neutral) → +1 (very positive)

Critical Distinctions for Exams

  • NPS scale is −100 to +100 (NOT 0 to 100): Detractors score the firm negatively, so NPS can go into large negative numbers. Don't confuse with CSAT (0–100%).
  • CLV:CAC 3:1 is the MINIMUM healthy benchmark — not the ideal. Healthy is 3–5:1. Below 3:1 needs immediate attention. Above 5:1 may mean underinvesting in growth.
  • Voluntary vs Involuntary Churn: Voluntary = active cancellation (competitor win, dissatisfaction). Involuntary = payment failure, expired card — addressable with dunning management.
  • CSAT vs CES use cases: CSAT = "did they like the interaction?" (holistic satisfaction). CES = "how hard was it?" (friction / effort). CES is actually a better predictor of churn than CSAT.
  • Revenue churn can be negative (good!): If existing customers are upgrading more than others are downgrading/churning — this is called "negative revenue churn" or "net revenue retention > 100%".
  • Journey map types: Service Blueprint is the only one that includes internal/operational processes. Current State = "as is". Future State = "to be". Day-in-Life = zooms out beyond brand touchpoints.

NP1A survey of NPS responses returns 55% Promoters, 30% Passives and 15% Detractors. What is the Net Promoter Score?
NPS = %Promoters − %Detractors = 55 − 15 = 40
Passives are excluded from the calculation. A score of +40 is solidly positive.
NP2A subscription service begins the quarter with 1,200 customers and loses 84 of them by quarter-end. What is the churn rate (%)?
Churn Rate = Customers Lost / Customers at Start × 100 = 84 / 1200 × 100 = 7%
7% of the starting base left during the quarter.
NP3A company reports a quarterly churn rate of 6.5%. What is its customer retention rate (%)?
Retention Rate = 100% − Churn Rate = 100 − 6.5 = 93.5%
Retention is simply the inverse of churn — keeping 93.5 of every 100 customers.
NP4A customer spends an average of ₹250 per order, orders 4 times a year, and stays with the brand for 3 years. Using the simple CLV = AOV × frequency × lifespan (no margin), what is the CLV?
CLV = AOV × Purchase Frequency × Lifespan = 250 × 4 × 3 = ₹3,000
This is gross revenue value; multiplying by profit margin would give the profit-based CLV.
NP5Using the full CLV formula CLV = AOV × frequency × lifespan × profit margin, with AOV = ₹250, frequency = 4/yr, lifespan = 3 yr and profit margin = 30%, what is the CLV?
CLV = AOV × Frequency × Lifespan × Profit Margin = 250 × 4 × 3 × 0.30 = 3000 × 0.30 = ₹900
Adding the 30% margin converts the ₹3,000 revenue figure into expected profit.
NP6A store generated ₹96,000 in revenue from 480 orders last month. What is the Average Order Value (AOV)?
AOV = Total Revenue / Number of Orders = 96,000 / 480 = ₹200
Each order is worth ₹200 on average — upselling raises this and lifts CLV.
NP7Using the discount-adjusted CLV model CLV = margin × r / (1 + d − r) with annual margin per customer = ₹600, retention rate r = 0.80 and discount rate d = 0.10, what is the CLV?
CLV = margin × r / (1 + d − r) = 600 × 0.80 / (1 + 0.10 − 0.80) = 600 × 0.80 / 0.30 = 600 × 2.6667 = ₹1,600
The retention/discount factor r/(1+d−r) = 0.80/0.30 ≈ 2.67 turns a single year's margin into lifetime value.
NP8Of 160 customers surveyed after checkout, 138 gave a top-box (satisfied) rating. What is the CSAT score (%)?
CSAT = Satisfied Customers / Total Responses × 100 = 138 / 160 × 100 = 86.25%
Above the 80% "excellent" benchmark mentioned in the study material.
NP9A business calculates a CLV of ₹4,500 per customer and a CAC of ₹900. What is the CLV-to-CAC ratio (express as the number, e.g. 5 for 5:1)?
CLV : CAC = CLV / CAC = 4500 / 900 = 5 (i.e. 5:1)
Well above the 3:1 industry benchmark — each ₹1 of acquisition spend returns ₹5 of lifetime value.

Q3 · 2 marks A customer spends an average of ₹800 per order, orders 3 times a year, and stays for 5 years. Using the simple CLV = AOV × frequency × lifespan (no margin), what is the CLV?

Q4 · 2 marks On the 0–10 "How likely are you to recommend us?" scale used for NPS, into which category does a respondent scoring 7 or 8 fall?
Promoters = 9–10 · Passives = 7–8 · Detractors = 0–6
Scores of 7–8 are Passives: satisfied but unenthusiastic. They are part of the total respondent base (the denominator) yet contribute nothing to NPS = %Promoters − %Detractors.
Q5 · 2 marks A SaaS firm has a CLV of ₹18,000 per customer and a Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) of ₹6,000. What does the resulting CLV:CAC ratio tell management?
CLV:CAC = 18,000 / 6,000 = 3.0 (3:1)
A 3:1 ratio is the widely cited "healthy" benchmark — each acquired customer returns three times their acquisition cost. Below 1:1 the firm loses money; far above 3:1 may signal under-investment in growth, not overspending.
Q6 · 2 marks A brand runs sentiment analysis on thousands of social-media mentions and product reviews. What does this technique primarily measure, and why is it valuable for customer analytics?
Sentiment analysis classifies text as positive / neutral / negative (polarity)
It mines unstructured text (reviews, tweets, support tickets) to gauge how customers feel at scale — an early warning of satisfaction shifts. It does not compute CLV or churn directly, nor is it merely mention-counting.
