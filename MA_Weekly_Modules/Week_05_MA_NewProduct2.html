1. Linear vs Non-Linear Pricing

Linear Pricing

In linear pricing, the price per unit stays constant regardless of quantity purchased. The total price scales proportionally with volume — simple, transparent, and predictable for consumers.

Total Price = Price per Unit × Quantity
Example: ₹50/unit × 10 units = ₹500 total
  • Constant marginal price — every additional unit costs the same
  • Easy for customers to understand and budget
  • Used widely in utilities (per kWh), SaaS per-seat licensing
  • Example: Netflix ₹649/month flat rate — stream as much or as little as you want
  • Psychological Pricing pairing: Linear pricing is often enhanced with psychological pricing (e.g., £9.99 instead of £10.00) to boost perceived value.
  • Tiered Pricing variant: Different price tiers for different quantity bands (e.g., wholesale or software-licence tiers) — attracts different customer segments and encourages higher sales volumes.

Non-Linear Pricing

In non-linear pricing, the price per unit varies with quantity. Firms use this to extract more consumer surplus and segment buyers by their willingness to pay.

XYZ Widgets — Demand Model & Profit Maximization

Let's walk through a complete worked example using a linear demand curve.

Given: Demand: Q = 250 − 3P  |  Marginal Cost (MC) = ₹0 (digital product simplification)
Inverse Demand: P = (250 − Q) / 3 = 83.33 − Q/3

Total Revenue: TR = P × Q = (83.33 − Q/3) × Q = 83.33Q − Q²/3

Marginal Revenue: MR = dTR/dQ = 83.33 − 2Q/3

Profit Max (MR = MC): 83.33 − 2Q/3 = 0
2Q/3 = 83.33
Q* = 125 units

Optimal Price: P* = 83.33 − 125/3 = 83.33 − 41.67 = ₹41.67

Total Revenue: TR* = 41.67 × 125 = ₹5,208.33

Consumer Surplus: CS = ½ × 125 × (83.33 − 41.67) = ½ × 125 × 41.66 = ₹2,603.75
Key Insight: The gap between the Demand curve and MR curve represents the "sacrifice" — to sell one more unit, the firm must lower price on ALL previous units (under uniform pricing). This is why MR falls twice as fast as demand.

Psychological Principles Driving Bundling

1. Cognitive Ease

One bundle decision is mentally simpler than evaluating many individual items — reduces decision fatigue.

2. Perceived Value

Bundle often "feels" worth more than the sum of parts — especially when components have different perceived values.

3. FOMO / Best Value

"Best value" framing triggers fear of missing out — customers feel they'd be irrational not to choose the bundle.

4. Anchoring

Listing individual prices first anchors the customer high; the bundle price then seems like a great deal.

3. Price Skimming vs Penetration Pricing

Price Skimming

Launch at HIGH price → gradually reduce

Best for: Innovative products, tech, luxury, early-adopter markets

  • Recover R&D / launch costs rapidly
  • Signal premium quality to market
  • Maximise revenue from early adopters (high WTP)
  • Create "deal" perception as price drops
iPhone 14 Launch:
Launch price: ₹79,900 → After 6 months: ₹69,900 → After 12 months: ₹59,900

Tesla Model S: Started at $130k, now offers base from $74k as production scaled

Challenges:

  • Attracts competitors at lower price points
  • Customer resentment (early adopters feel cheated)
  • Limited initial market size (small early adopter segment)

Penetration Pricing

Launch at LOW price → gain market share fast

Best for: Price-sensitive markets, commodity products, building user base, network-effect businesses

  • Rapid mass adoption and market penetration
  • Economies of scale drive down costs
  • Network effects kick in (more users = more value)
  • Lock competitors out of market
Jio's 2016 Launch:
Free data + calls for 6 months → 100M users in 170 days (fastest ever). Then ₹149/month.

Netflix India: Launched at ₹499/month (vs ₹800 global) → Mobile-only ₹149/month to penetrate price-sensitive segment

Challenges:

  • Price wars — competitors match your low price
  • Perceived as low quality ("cheap = bad")
  • Very hard to raise prices later (customer backlash)

4. Markdown Pricing

Markdown Formulas

Markdown Amount = Original Price − New (Sale) Price

Markdown % = (Original Price − New Price) / Original Price × 100

New Price = Original Price × (1 − Markdown%/100)

Worked Example — Seasonal Footwear

Scenario: High-street shoe brand runs end-of-season clearance

ItemOriginal PriceSale PriceMarkdown ₹Markdown %
Running Shoes₹4,000₹2,800₹1,20030%
Formal Loafers₹6,500₹3,900₹2,60040%
Sandals₹1,800₹900₹90050%

Running Shoes calculation: Markdown% = (4000−2800)/4000 × 100 = 1200/4000 × 100 = 30%

Types of Markdowns

Permanent Markdowns
  • Actual cost reduction passed to customer
  • Clearance of discontinued lines
  • Damage or obsolescence pricing
  • New model supersedes old
Promotional Markdowns
  • Temporary sale event pricing
  • Festival / holiday discounts
  • Weekly specials, flash sales
  • Price returns to original after event

Markdown Calendar Strategy — 3-Week Clearance

Retailers use a staged markdown approach to systematically clear slow-moving inventory while optimising total revenue recovery:

20%
Week 1

Move slow-moving inventory; price-sensitive early shoppers respond. Target: sell 50% of excess stock.

40%
Week 2

Deeper discount attracts bargain hunters. Target: sell remaining 80% of remaining stock.

60%
Week 3

Final clearance — avoid carrying costs. Better to liquidate than store. Target: 100% clearance.

Goal of Markdown Strategy: Avoid inventory carrying costs (storage, insurance, capital tied up) and dead stock. ₹500 recovered at deep discount beats ₹0 from unsold items plus ₹200 carrying cost.

5. Price Optimization

Core Pricing Methodologies

MethodFormulaUse Case
Cost-PlusPrice = Total Cost × (1 + Markup%)Manufacturing, B2B contracts, retail
Value-BasedPrice = Max WTP of target segmentSaaS, consulting, premium brands
CompetitivePrice relative to competitor (±X%)Commodity markets, retail
Target ReturnPrice = (Cost + Target Profit) / UnitsNew product launch, cost recovery

Price Elasticity of Demand

Price Elasticity (E) = % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Price

E = (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔP/P) = (ΔQ/ΔP) × (P/Q)

Note: E is always negative (price up → demand down) by law of demand
ConditionValueInterpretationExamples
ElasticE < −1 (e.g., −2.5)% drop in demand exceeds % price rise — price sensitiveLuxury goods, restaurant meals, holidays
Inelastic−1 < E < 0 (e.g., −0.3)% drop in demand less than % price rise — insensitiveInsulin, petrol, cigarettes, salt
Unit ElasticE = −1% change in demand exactly equals % price changeTheoretical midpoint
Worked Example: Clothing retailer raises price 10%, demand falls 15%
E = −15% / 10% = −1.5 → Elastic demand (customers are price sensitive)
Implication: Revenue falls when price rises — this price increase was a bad decision.

Counter-example: Petrol station raises price 10%, demand falls 2%
E = −2% / 10% = −0.2 → Inelastic (people still need fuel)
Implication: Revenue rises when price rises — petrol companies can increase prices profitably.

A/B Testing for Pricing Decisions

z = (p₁ − p₂) / √[ p̂(1−p̂)(1/n₁ + 1/n₂) ]

where p̂ = (x₁ + x₂) / (n₁ + n₂) [pooled proportion]
If |z| > 1.96 → statistically significant at α = 0.05 (95% confidence)
Pricing A/B Test:
Control (Group A): Price = ₹999/month → n₁ = 500, conversions = 40 → p₁ = 8%
Treatment (Group B): Price = ₹899/month → n₂ = 500, conversions = 55 → p₂ = 11%

p̂ = (40+55)/(500+500) = 95/1000 = 0.095
z = (0.11−0.08) / √[0.095×0.905×(1/500+1/500)]
z = 0.03 / √[0.08598 × 0.004] = 0.03 / √0.000344 = 0.03 / 0.01854 = 1.62

Since |z| = 1.62 < 1.96 → Not statistically significant at 95% — need larger sample or stronger effect.

Machine Learning for Price Optimization

Regression Models

Predict revenue/demand as a function of price + contextual features (season, competition, day of week). Use to find optimal price point.

Decision Trees

Segment customers by price sensitivity automatically. Create price rules: "high-income customers in metro cities → premium price".

Reinforcement Learning

Dynamic pricing agent that learns from market response in real-time. Amazon changes prices ~2.5 million times/day using RL-based systems.

7. Summary & Exam Tips

Key Formulas — Revision Card

1. Inverse Demand: P = a − bQ (from Q = a/b − P/b)
2. Marginal Revenue: MR = a − 2bQ (slope twice steeper than demand)
3. Profit Max: MR = MC → solve for Q*, then find P*
4. Consumer Surplus: CS = ½ × Q* × (P_max − P*)
5. Markdown %: (Original − Sale) / Original × 100
6. Price Elasticity: E = %ΔQ / %ΔP [negative by definition]
7. A/B Test z-stat: z = (p₁−p₂) / √[p̂(1−p̂)(1/n₁+1/n₂)]
8. Cost-Plus Price: Price = Cost × (1 + Markup%)

Critical Distinctions for Exams

  • Price Skimming ≠ Price Discrimination: Skimming drops prices over time for everyone; price discrimination charges different segments simultaneously different prices.
  • Pure Bundling vs Mixed Bundling: Pure = bundle only (no individual sales); Mixed = bundle + individual items both available.
  • Elastic vs Inelastic — Revenue Rule: Elastic → lower price increases revenue. Inelastic → higher price increases revenue.
  • MR always slopes twice as steeply as linear demand — if demand is P = a−bQ, then MR = a−2bQ.
  • Two-Part Tariff: Fixed fee + per-unit. If firm charges per-unit = MC and sets fixed fee = consumer surplus, it extracts maximum profit.
  • A/B Test significance: z > 1.96 for 95% CI, z > 2.58 for 99% CI. Small samples → use t-test instead.

NP1A jacket originally priced at ₹1,200 is put on sale for ₹900. What is the markdown percentage?
Markdown % = (Original − Sale) / Original × 100 = (1200 − 900) / 1200 × 100 = 300 / 1200 × 100 = 25%
The price was cut by a quarter of its original value.
NP2A product sells for ₹80 and costs ₹52 to produce. What is the profit margin % (margin on selling price)?
Margin % = (Price − Cost) / Price × 100 = (80 − 52) / 80 × 100 = 28 / 80 × 100 = 35%
Note: markup (on cost) would be 28/52 = 53.8% — margin always uses price as the base.
NP3Fixed costs are ₹54,000, the selling price is ₹25/unit and the variable cost is ₹16/unit. How many units must be sold to break even?
Break-even units = Fixed Cost / (Price − Variable Cost) = 54,000 / (25 − 16) = 54,000 / 9 = 6,000 units
Each unit contributes ₹9 toward covering the fixed costs.
NP4When the price of a product is cut from ₹50 to ₹45, weekly quantity sold rises from 4,000 to 4,600 units. Compute the price elasticity of demand (signed value, 1 dp).
%ΔQ = (4600 − 4000)/4000 × 100 = +15% %ΔP = (45 − 50)/50 × 100 = −10% E = %ΔQ / %ΔP = 15 / (−10) = −1.5
|E| = 1.5 > 1 ⇒ demand is elastic; cutting price raised total revenue here.
NP5Three items priced individually at ₹45, ₹30 and ₹15 are offered as a bundle for ₹72. What is the bundle discount % versus the sum of parts?
Sum of parts = 45 + 30 + 15 = ₹90 Discount % = (90 − 72) / 90 × 100 = 18 / 90 × 100 = 20%
Mixed bundling anchors the ₹90 reference price so the ₹72 bundle feels like a deal.
NP6Under a linear pricing model the price is ₹12.50 per unit regardless of quantity. What is the total price for an order of 340 units?
Total Price = Price per Unit × Quantity = 12.50 × 340 = ₹4,250
Linear pricing keeps the per-unit price constant — no bulk discount applies.
NP7A product has the demand function Q = 300 − 4P. At a price of P = ₹30, what is the total revenue (R = P × Q)?
Q = 300 − 4P = 300 − 4(30) = 300 − 120 = 180 units R = P × Q = 30 × 180 = ₹5,400
Plugging the chosen price into the demand curve gives quantity, then revenue.
NP8An item costs ₹40 and is priced at ₹64. What is the markup % (markup on cost)?
Markup % = (Price − Cost) / Cost × 100 = (64 − 40) / 40 × 100 = 24 / 40 × 100 = 60%
Markup uses cost as the base (60%); the same spread expressed as margin on price is 24/64 = 37.5%.
NP9A start-up launches an innovative gadget at a high initial price, targeting early adopters, and plans to lower it gradually as competition arrives. Which pricing strategy is this?
High initial price for early adopters, lowered over time = price skimming. Penetration pricing does the opposite — a low launch price to grab market share quickly.

Q1 · 2 marks When a product's price is reduced from ₹80 to ₹72, weekly quantity sold rises from 5,000 to 5,900 units. What is the price elasticity of demand (signed, 1 dp)?
%ΔQ = (5900 − 5000)/5000 × 100 = +18% %ΔP = (72 − 80)/80 × 100 = −10% E = %ΔQ / %ΔP = 18 / (−10) = −1.8
|E| = 1.8 > 1, so demand is elastic — the price cut raised total revenue. (−1.0 mistakes the ratio for unit elasticity; −2.5 swaps numerator and denominator.)
Q2 · 2 marks Fixed costs are ₹90,000, the selling price is ₹45/unit and the variable cost is ₹27/unit. How many units must be sold to break even?
Break-even units = Fixed Cost / (Price − Variable Cost) = 90,000 / (45 − 27) = 90,000 / 18 = 5,000 units
Each unit contributes ₹18 toward fixed costs. (3,333 wrongly divides by price; 2,000 divides by price+VC.)
Q3 · 2 marks A product sells for ₹150 and costs ₹90 to produce. What is the profit margin % (margin on selling price)?
Margin % = (Price − Cost) / Price × 100 = (150 − 90) / 150 × 100 = 60 / 150 × 100 = 40%
Markup on cost would be 60/90 = 66.7% — margin always uses price as the base, so the two figures differ.
Q4 · 2 marks A firm launching a premium smartwatch sets a deliberately high introductory price to capture early adopters, then lowers it over time as competition grows. Which pricing strategy is this, and what is its primary goal?
High launch price → skim → lower over time = price skimming
Skimming targets inelastic early adopters to recover development costs and earn high early margins. Penetration does the opposite (low price for fast share). Cost-plus and loss-leader describe unrelated mechanisms.
Q5 · 2 marks A retailer's demand for a luxury good is found to be inelastic (|E| < 1). To increase total revenue, what should the firm do, and why?
Inelastic (|E| < 1): %ΔQ < %ΔP ⇒ raise price → revenue rises
When demand is inelastic, a price increase loses proportionally fewer units than the price gain, so total revenue (P × Q) rises. Cutting price would reduce revenue. The mirror rule: with elastic demand, cut price to raise revenue.
Q6 · 2 marks A software company offers its word processor, spreadsheet and presentation apps together as one suite at a single combined price rather than selling each separately. What is the main economic rationale for this bundling?
Bundle price < Σ standalone prices, but reservation prices for the apps are negatively correlated → bundle extracts more total surplus
Bundling works because customers value the components differently; combining them lets the firm sell to buyers whose willingness-to-pay for any single item would be too low, capturing more aggregate consumer surplus. It does not raise per-item margins, fix accounting, or remove competition.
