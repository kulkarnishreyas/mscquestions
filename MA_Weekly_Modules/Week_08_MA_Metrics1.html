1. Channel Management Metrics

Six Categories of Channel Metrics

Channel metrics measure the performance and profitability of your distribution and marketing channels (direct, indirect, digital, partner, retail). They fall into 6 categories:

Financial Metrics

  • ROCI (Return on Channel Investment)
  • Channel Margin
  • Revenue per Channel

Performance Metrics

  • Conversion Rate
  • Win Rate
  • Deals Closed

Relationship Metrics

  • Partner Retention Rate (CRR)
  • Partner Satisfaction (NPS)
  • Partner Engagement Score

Operational Metrics

  • Sales Growth Rate
  • Average Order Value (AOV)
  • Order Fulfilment Time

Market Metrics

  • Market Share by Channel
  • Coverage Ratio
  • Territory Penetration

Sales Metrics

  • Sales per Channel Partner
  • Lead Conversion by Channel
  • Pipeline Velocity

Core Channel Formulas

ROCI (%) = (Net Channel Profit / Total Channel Investment) × 100
Where: Net Channel Profit = Revenue Generated − Cost of Goods − Channel Investment
Channel Margin (%) = (Revenue − Channel Costs) / Revenue × 100
Conversion Rate (%) = (Sales / Total Leads) × 100
Partner Retention Rate (CRR) = (Retained Partners / Total Partners at Start) × 100
Sales Growth Rate (%) = [(Current Period Revenue − Prior Period Revenue) / Prior Period Revenue] × 100
Coverage Ratio (%) = (Regions Reached / Total Target Regions) × 100

Worked Numerical Problems

Problem 1 — ROCI Calculation:
Channel Investment: ₹1,50,000
Revenue Generated by Channel: ₹2,00,000
Net Profit (after investment, simplified): ₹2,00,000 − ₹1,50,000 = ₹50,000

ROCI = (50,000 / 1,50,000) × 100 = 33.3%
Interpretation: For every ₹100 invested in this channel, ₹33 in profit is returned.
Problem 2 — Partner Retention Rate:
Partners at start of year: 120
Partners who left: 40
Retained Partners: 120 − 40 = 80

CRR = (80 / 120) × 100 = 66.67%
Interpretation: The company retained two-thirds of its partners. Industry benchmark is typically >80% for healthy partnerships.
Problem 3 — Sales Conversion Rate:
Total Leads generated: 400
Converted to Sales: 100

Conversion Rate = (100 / 400) × 100 = 25%
Interpretation: 1 in 4 leads converts to a sale. Compare against industry average to assess channel efficiency.
Problem 4 — Market Penetration:
Target market size: 10,000 potential customers
Current customers: 2,000

Penetration = (2,000 / 10,000) × 100 = 20%
Interpretation: 80% of the target market is still untapped — significant growth opportunity.

Channel A ROCI Verification:
Net Profit = ₹5,00,000 × 40% margin = ₹2,00,000 − wait, simpler:
ROCI = (Revenue × Margin) / Investment = (5,00,000 × 0.40) / 5,00,000 — No.
Using ROCI = Net Profit / Investment:
Net Profit = ₹5,00,000 − (5,00,000 × 0.60) − ₹2,50,000 = ₹2,50,000 − ₹2,50,000 = ₹0 — Let's use margin on investment:
Margin % of investment = (₹2,00,000 / ₹5,00,000) × 100 = 40% = ROCI 40% (net revenue ratio to investment).

2. Advertising Effectiveness Metrics

Core Advertising Metrics

CTR — Click-Through Rate

CTR (%) = (Clicks / Impressions) × 100
2,000 clicks on 50,000 impressions
CTR = (2,000 / 50,000) × 100 = 4%
ChannelAvg CTR Benchmark
Google Search2–3%
Google Display0.1%
Social Media0.5–2%
Email2–5%

CPC — Cost Per Click

CPC = Total Ad Spend / Total Clicks
Ad Spend: ₹10,000  |  Clicks: 20,000
CPC = 10,000 / 20,000 = ₹0.50

CPM — Cost Per Thousand Impressions

CPM = (Total Cost / Total Impressions) × 1,000
Cost: ₹1,500  |  Impressions: 50,000
CPM = (1,500 / 50,000) × 1,000 = ₹30

Conversion & Return Metrics

CPA — Cost Per Acquisition

CPA = Total Ad Spend / Total Conversions
Spend: ₹20,000  |  Conversions: 1,000
CPA = 20,000 / 1,000 = ₹20

CPA should be compared against CLV. If CLV > CPA, the acquisition is profitable.

ROAS — Return on Ad Spend

ROAS = Revenue from Ads / Ad Spend
Revenue: ₹3,00,000  |  Spend: ₹1,00,000
ROAS = 3,00,000 / 1,00,000 = 3.0×
Every ₹1 spent generates ₹3 in revenue.

Ad ROI — Return on Investment

Ad ROI (%) = (Revenue − Ad Spend) / Ad Spend × 100
Revenue: ₹5,00,000  |  Spend: ₹2,00,000
Ad ROI = (3,00,000 / 2,00,000) × 100 = 150%

Critical Distinction: ROAS = 5,00,000/2,00,000 = 2.5×. ROAS does not subtract the spend from the numerator; ROI does. They are different numbers.

Brand Lift

Brand Lift (%) = [(Post-campaign Awareness − Pre-campaign Awareness) / Pre-campaign Awareness] × 100
Pre-campaign awareness: 40%
Post-campaign awareness: 52%
Brand Lift = (52 − 40) / 40 × 100 = 30%

Brand Lift measures the awareness increase attributable to the campaign, measured via controlled surveys.

Video-Specific Metrics

CPCV (Cost per Completed View) = Ad Spend / Completed Video Views
CPCV Comparison:
Social Video: ₹30,000 spend / 10,000 completed views = ₹3.00 CPCV
YouTube Pre-roll: ₹40,000 spend / 8,000 completed views = ₹5.00 CPCV
Winner: Social Video (lower cost per engaged viewer)

3. Attribution Models (Deep Dive)

What is Attribution?

Attribution is the process of assigning credit to marketing touchpoints that contributed to a conversion. Different models distribute credit differently, leading to very different conclusions about which channels are valuable.

Customer Journey Example:
Day 1: Sees Social Media Ad → Day 3: Clicks Email Campaign → Day 5: Clicks Google Search Ad → Day 7: Purchases ₹2,000

Which channel gets credit for the ₹2,000 sale? It depends on the attribution model.

The 5 Core Attribution Models

1. First-Click Attribution Awareness Focus

100% of credit goes to the first touchpoint in the journey.

Social Ad: 100% Email: 0% Search: 0%

Pro: Identifies which channels drive discovery and brand awareness.
Con: Completely ignores nurturing and closing touchpoints. Overvalues top-of-funnel.

2. Last-Click Attribution Closing Focus

100% of credit goes to the last touchpoint before conversion. (Google Analytics default historically.)

Social Ad: 0% Email: 0% Search: 100%

Pro: Simple; focuses budget on the conversion-driving channel.
Con: Undervalues awareness and nurturing campaigns. Biases spend toward bottom-of-funnel.

3. Linear Attribution Balanced View

Equal credit distributed across all touchpoints.

Social Ad: 33.3% Email: 33.3% Search: 33.3%

Pro: Acknowledges that all touchpoints played a role.
Con: Treats all touchpoints as equally important, which is rarely true.

4. Time Decay Attribution Recency Weighted

More credit to touchpoints closer to the conversion date (exponential decay).

Social Ad (Day 1): ~10% Email (Day 3): ~20% Search (Day 5): ~30% Direct (Day 7): ~40%

Pro: Reflects that recent interactions are more relevant to purchase decision.
Con: Systematically undervalues brand awareness and top-of-funnel investment.

5. U-Shaped (Position-Based) Attribution Lead Gen Focus

40% to first touch, 40% to last touch, 20% split across middle touchpoints.

Social Ad: 40% Email: 20% Search: 40%

Pro: Values both acquisition (who brought the customer in) and closing (who closed the deal).
Con: The 40/40/20 split is somewhat arbitrary; middle-journey nurturing is undervalued.

Attribution Model Comparison Table

Exam Scenario: A marketer using Last-Click attribution sees that "Google Search" is the top-performing channel and cuts Social Media budget. But Social Media was driving discovery (First-Click). Cutting it would reduce the pipeline feeding into Search conversions. This is the fundamental attribution model trap.

5. Market Share Analysis

Market Share Formulas

Market Share (%) = (Company Sales / Total Market Sales) × 100
Relative Market Share = Company's Market Share / Largest Competitor's Market Share
Share of Voice (SOV) = (Company Ad Spend / Total Industry Ad Spend) × 100
Example — FoodTech Market:
Total FoodTech market: ₹50,00,000 revenue
Our company sales: ₹5,00,000

Market Share = (5,00,000 / 50,00,000) × 100 = 10%
Relative Market Share:
Our company: 10% market share
Market leader: 25% market share

Relative MS = 10 / 25 = 0.40
Interpretation: We have 40% of the leader's market share. In BCG Matrix, Relative MS >1 = market leader.

Share of Voice (SOV) Strategy

SituationMeaningStrategy Implication
SOV > Market ShareInvesting above your weightBuilding brand; aggressive growth phase
SOV = Market ShareMaintaining positionDefending market share; stable
SOV < Market ShareUnder-investing in shareRisk of losing share to more aggressive competitors
Example — SOV Calculation:
Total industry ad spend: ₹1,00,00,000
Our company ad spend: ₹12,00,000
SOV = (12,00,000 / 1,00,00,000) × 100 = 12%
Our Market Share = 10%

SOV (12%) > Market Share (10%) → We are investing above our share → Pursuing aggressive growth strategy.

7. Summary & Exam Tips

Master Formula Sheet — Week 8

CTR (%) = (Clicks / Impressions) × 100
CPC = Ad Spend / Clicks
CPA = Ad Spend / Conversions
CPM = (Ad Spend / Impressions) × 1,000
ROAS = Revenue / Ad Spend
Ad ROI (%) = (Revenue − Spend) / Spend × 100
Brand Lift (%) = (Post − Pre) / Pre × 100
ROCI (%) = Net Profit / Investment × 100
Market Share (%) = (Company Sales / Total Market Sales) × 100
Relative MS = Our MS / Leader's MS  |  SOV (%) = Our Ad Spend / Industry Ad Spend × 100

Exam Tips

  • ROAS ≠ ROI (critical exam distinction): ROAS = Revenue / Spend (raw ratio, no subtraction). ROI = (Revenue − Spend) / Spend × 100 (profit-based %). If Revenue=₹500K, Spend=₹100K: ROAS=5.0, ROI=400%. They are always different numbers.
  • CPM multiplies by 1,000 — always multiply by 1000, not divide. CPM = (Cost/Impressions)×1000.
  • Attribution models on exam: U-shaped = 40/20/40; Linear = equal; First-Click = 100% first; Last-Click = 100% last; Time Decay = weighted toward recent.
  • Brand Lift is relative, not absolute: Brand Lift % = (change / pre) × 100, not just the absolute percentage point change.
  • SOV > Market Share = investing for growth; SOV < Market Share = defending / at risk. Memorise this rule.
  • Relative Market Share >1 = market leader. <1 = challenger/follower.
  • Display Ads always have low CTR (benchmark ~0.1%) — don't be alarmed by 0.16% CTR, but high CPA is the real red flag.
  • ROCI for channel investment uses Net Profit (not Revenue) in the numerator — different from ROAS which uses Revenue.

NP1A display ad records 40,000 impressions and 1,400 clicks. What is the Click-Through Rate (CTR, %)?
CTR = (Clicks / Impressions) × 100 = (1,400 / 40,000) × 100 = 3.5%
3.5% of viewers clicked — a healthy display-ad engagement rate.
NP2A campaign spends ₹7,200 and earns 4,500 clicks. What is the Cost Per Click (CPC)?
CPC = Total Spend / Number of Clicks = 7,200 / 4,500 = ₹1.60 per click
A lower CPC means more clicks for the same budget.
NP3An ad costs ₹8,400 to deliver 350,000 impressions. What is the CPM (cost per 1,000 impressions)?
CPM = (Cost / Impressions) × 1,000 = (8,400 / 350,000) × 1,000 = 0.024 × 1,000 = ₹24
It costs ₹24 to show this ad to a thousand people.
NP4A campaign spends ₹24,000 and produces 600 conversions. What is the Cost Per Acquisition (CPA)?
CPA = Total Spend / Number of Conversions = 24,000 / 600 = ₹40 per acquisition
Each new customer cost ₹40 to acquire through this campaign.
NP5A campaign generates ₹84,000 in revenue on an ad spend of ₹21,000. What is the ROAS (Return on Ad Spend, as a multiple)?
ROAS = Revenue from Campaign / Total Ad Spend = 84,000 / 21,000 = 4× (4:1)
Every ₹1 of ad spend returned ₹4 in revenue.
NP6Using the same campaign (revenue ₹84,000, ad spend ₹21,000), what is the Advertising ROI (%) = (Revenue − Spend) / Spend × 100?
Ad ROI % = (Revenue − Spend) / Spend × 100 = (84,000 − 21,000) / 21,000 × 100 = 63,000 / 21,000 × 100 = 300%
ROAS of 4× equals an ROI of 300% (the profit is 3× the spend).
NP7A channel investment of ₹120,000 yields a net channel profit of ₹36,000. What is the Return on Channel Investment (ROCI, %)?
ROCI = (Net Channel Profit / Total Channel Investment) × 100 = (36,000 / 120,000) × 100 = 30%
The channel returned 30% on the capital invested in it.
NP8A brand records ₹7.2 million in sales in a market with total sales of ₹48 million. What is its market share (%)?
Market Share = (Company Sales / Total Market Sales) × 100 = (7.2M / 48M) × 100 = 15%
The brand captures 15 of every 100 rupees spent in the market.
NP9A brand-lift study finds aided brand awareness rose from a pre-campaign 32% to a post-campaign 44%. What is the brand lift (%)?
Brand Lift % = (Post − Pre) / Pre × 100 = (44 − 32) / 32 × 100 = 12 / 32 × 100 = 37.5%
Awareness grew by 37.5% relative to its pre-campaign baseline (a 12-point absolute rise).
NP10A landing page receives 9,000 visitors, of whom 360 complete a purchase. What is the conversion rate (%)?
Conversion Rate = (Conversions / Total Visitors) × 100 = (360 / 9,000) × 100 = 4%
4 in every 100 visitors converted to a sale.
NP11Your brand holds a 18% market share; the market leader holds 30%. What is your relative market share (your share ÷ leader's share)?
Relative Market Share = Our Share / Leader's Share = 18 / 30 = 0.6×
A value below 1 means you are a challenger, not the leader; 0.6 means you are 60% the size of the leader.
NP12Campaign A has a ROAS of 4× and Campaign B a ROAS of 2.5×, on equal spend. Which interpretation is correct?
A higher ROAS is better: Campaign A returns ₹4 per ₹1 spent vs ₹2.5 for B, so Campaign A is more efficient. Any ROAS > 1 means revenue exceeds spend.

📝 Exam-Style MCQs — 2 Marks Each

Each question is worth 2 marks — a mix of applied numerical and conceptual items. Click an option to check it; the correct answer and explanation appear instantly.

Q1 · 2 marks A banner campaign costs ₹9,600 to deliver 6,00,000 impressions. What is the CPM (cost per 1,000 impressions)?
CPM = (Cost / Impressions) × 1,000 = (9,600 / 6,00,000) × 1,000 = 0.016 × 1,000 = ₹16
It costs ₹16 to show this ad to a thousand people. (₹0.016 forgets the ×1,000; ₹62.50 inverts the ratio.)
Q2 · 2 marks A campaign earns ₹1,35,000 in attributable revenue on an ad spend of ₹27,000. What is the ROAS (Return on Ad Spend, as a multiple)?
ROAS = Revenue from Campaign / Total Ad Spend = 1,35,000 / 27,000 = 5× (5:1)
Every ₹1 of ad spend returned ₹5 in revenue. (The equivalent Ad ROI would be (1,35,000 − 27,000)/27,000 = 400%.)
Q3 · 2 marks A brand's product sales were ₹4.5 crore in a market with total category sales of ₹25 crore. What is the brand's market share?
Market Share % = (Brand Sales / Total Market Sales) × 100 = (4.5 / 25) × 100 = 18%
The brand holds 18% of category sales. (5.56% inverts the ratio; 82% is the rivals' combined share.)
Q4 · 2 marks A marketing manager reports a campaign delivered a ROAS of 5× and an Ad ROI of 400%. Why are these two figures different despite using the same revenue and spend?
ROAS = Revenue / Spend = 5× Ad ROI = (Revenue − Spend) / Spend × 100 = (5 − 1) × 100 = 400%
ROAS counts gross revenue per rupee spent; ROI nets out the spend first, so it is always lower by exactly 100 percentage points (one "unit" of spend). They are consistent, not contradictory.
Q5 · 2 marks A customer saw a display ad, later clicked a paid-search ad, and finally converted after clicking an email link. Under a last-touch attribution model, which channel receives 100% of the conversion credit?
Last-touch → 100% credit to the final touchpoint = the email
Last-touch credits only the channel immediately preceding conversion. First-touch would credit the display ad; linear would split credit equally (33% each); U-shaped would weight the first and last touches most heavily.
Q6 · 2 marks A media plan reports a brand's Share of Voice (SOV) at 30% while its market share is only 18%. What does this gap most usefully signal?
SOV = brand's share of total category ad spend / voice Market share = brand's share of category SALES
SOV measures advertising presence; market share measures actual sales. An "excess SOV" (30% voice vs 18% sales) is a classic leading indicator that the brand is investing ahead of its position, which tends to drive future share gains — not a guarantee of immediate parity.
