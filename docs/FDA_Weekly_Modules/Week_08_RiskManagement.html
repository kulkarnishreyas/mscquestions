2️⃣ Risk Measurement Techniques

Three VaR Methods

MethodHowPros/Cons
Historical SimulationRe-applies past returnsNo distribution assumption; assumes past repeats
Parametric (Var-Cov)Normal distribution + mean/SDFast; underestimates tail risk
Monte CarloSimulates many random scenariosFlexible; compute-heavy

Sharpe Ratio

Sharpe = (Rp − Rf) / σp
import numpy as np daily_returns = np.random.normal(0.001, 0.02, 252) risk_free = 0.0002 excess = daily_returns - risk_free sharpe = excess.mean() / excess.std() print(f"Sharpe: {sharpe:.4f}")

Role in Investment Strategy

  • Aligns portfolio with risk tolerance
  • Drives diversification across asset classes
  • Enables risk-adjusted return comparisons
  • Supports active management (hedging, rebalancing)
  • Reduces emotional decision-making

3️⃣ Value at Risk (VaR)

VaR = maximum expected loss over a defined period at a given confidence level.

Parametric VaR Formula

VaR = Zα × σ × √t

For 95% confidence: Z = 1.65; 99% confidence: Z = 2.33.

import numpy as np z_score = 1.65 # 95% std_dev = 0.02 # daily SD var = z_score * std_dev * np.sqrt(1) print(f"1-day VaR: {var:.2%}") # ~3.3%

Historical Example

₹17 crore portfolio. Sort 100 days of returns. 10th worst = −0.75%.
VaR (90% confidence) = 0.75% × 17 crore = ₹12,75,000

Limitations of VaR

📊 Normality Assumption

Real markets have fat tails — VaR underestimates extremes

📷 Static Snapshot

Ignores portfolio changes during horizon

👁️ Tail Risk Blindness

No info beyond confidence level

Conditional VaR (CVaR / Expected Shortfall)

Average loss beyond the VaR threshold — gives a deeper look into tail risk.

Applications

  • Regulatory compliance (Basel)
  • Hedging decisions
  • Asset management & capital allocation
  • Stress testing (combined with extreme scenarios)

4️⃣ Stress Testing

Simulates extreme but plausible scenarios to evaluate resilience.

Scenario Types

📚 Historical

Replay 2008 crisis, COVID-19 etc.

🔮 Hypothetical

Sudden rate hikes, inflation spikes

🔄 Reverse Stress Test

"What breaks us?" Work backwards from failure

Effectiveness Factors

  1. Granularity — tailor scenarios to institution's specifics
  2. Historical Benchmarking — track risk profile evolution
  3. Quantitative + Qualitative Validation

Implications

  • Capital buffer recalibration
  • Liquidity & asset allocation adjustments
  • Operational/technological improvements

Comparison of Approaches

ApproachDescription
Top-DownMacroeconomic, system-level (regulators)
Bottom-UpAsset/portfolio-level (institutional)
StaticFixed assumptions, snapshot
DynamicEvolving scenarios with feedback
MacroprudentialSystem stability (regulators)
MicroprudentialIndividual firm exposure

5️⃣ Credit Risk Analysis

Probability of borrower defaulting on obligations.

Quantitative Models

  • Logistic Regression — default vs repayment
  • GBM/SVM/Neural Networks — complex risk patterns

Credit Scoring

ApproachDescription
FICO ScorePayment history, debt, length, mix
Behavioural ScoringDynamic based on post-loan behaviour

Key Risk Factors

👤 Borrower-Specific

Income, DTI, credit history

🌍 Macroeconomic

Recession, inflation, rates

🏭 Industry-Specific

Cyclical sectors (tourism, mfg) more vulnerable

Mitigation Strategies

  • Credit limits per profile
  • Collateral & security
  • Diversify loan book across sectors
  • Stress testing under downturn scenarios

Real-World Cases

  • 2008 Subprime Crisis — over-optimistic MBS models
  • COVID-19 — surge in default rates
  • AI Credit Monitoring — real-time risk reassessment
  • Fintech (Upstart, Affirm) — alt data (education, spending) for inclusive scoring

7️⃣ Real-World Case Studies

JPMorgan "London Whale" (2012)

  • $6.2B loss from speculative derivative trades
  • Weak oversight; flawed risk models
  • Triggered regulatory scrutiny & internal reforms

Wells Fargo Account Fraud (2016)

  • Unauthorised accounts opened by employees under aggressive sales targets
  • Operational risk + reputational damage
  • Reformed incentive structures post-scandal

2008 Financial Crisis

  • Over-reliance on risk models that ignored tail events
  • Excessive leverage + housing-market collapse
  • Birthed Basel III, Dodd-Frank

COVID-19 (2020)

  • Static models failed against unprecedented volatility
  • Highlighted need for adaptive, scenario-based risk frameworks

Quantitative vs Qualitative Risk Approaches

Precise, measurableCatches non-numerical risks

Best practice: combine both.

📝 Summary & Exam Tips

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • 4-step pipeline: Identify → Assess → Mitigate → Monitor
  • Risk types: Market, Credit, Operational, Liquidity, Reputational, Emerging
  • Frameworks: ERM, ISO 31000, COSO, Basel
  • VaR = max expected loss at confidence; methods: Historical, Parametric, Monte Carlo
  • CVaR / ES = avg loss beyond VaR (better for tail risk)
  • Stress testing: historical, hypothetical, reverse
  • Credit risk via logistic/GBM models + FICO/behavioural scoring
  • Market risk drivers: rates, equity, FX, commodities, spreads
  • Combine quant (VaR, stress) + qualitative (oversight, ethics) for resilience
📌 Pitfalls:
  • Treating VaR as worst case — it's not (tail risk lies beyond)
  • Normality assumption in parametric VaR fails in crises
  • Single-scenario stress test misses systemic risk
  • Ignoring qualitative risks (culture, governance)
