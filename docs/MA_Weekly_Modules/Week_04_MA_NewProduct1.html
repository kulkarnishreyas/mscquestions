1. Product Attributes

What Are Product Attributes?

Product attributes are the characteristics and features that define a product and create value for consumers. Understanding attributes is fundamental to new product decisions because they determine how consumers perceive, compare, and choose among competing products.

Different attribute types serve different roles in the value proposition and consumer decision-making process.

Types of Product Attributes

1Core Attributes

The essential, non-negotiable functions. Without these, the product fails its basic purpose.

Example: A smartphone must be able to make calls; a car must transport people.

2Tangible Attributes

Observable, physical characteristics that can be measured objectively.

Examples: Screen size (6.1 inches), weight (174g), battery capacity (3,227 mAh), colour options.

3Intangible Attributes

Subjective qualities not directly measurable. Often drive premium pricing.

Examples: Brand prestige, warranty support quality, perceived durability, after-sales service.

4Functional Attributes

What the product does — its capabilities and performance dimensions.

Examples: Camera megapixels, processor speed, water resistance rating (IP68), zoom capability.

5Aesthetic Attributes

Sensory appeal — how the product looks, feels, sounds, and is experienced.

Examples: Industrial design language, material texture (glass vs. metal vs. plastic), display colour accuracy.

6Symbolic Attributes

What the product says about the owner — identity and social signalling.

Examples: Owning an iPhone signals being part of a premium ecosystem; a Tesla signals environmental values + status.

Product Attribute Pyramid

Core attributes are table stakes; symbolic attributes create emotional loyalty and premium pricing power.

2. Conjoint Analysis

Purpose & Overview

Conjoint analysis determines which product attributes drive consumer preference and how consumers make trade-offs between attributes. It is the gold-standard technique for new product design and pricing research.

  • Part Worth Utilities: Numerical values representing how much each attribute level adds to overall preference
  • Market Share Simulation: Use utilities to predict market share of hypothetical products
  • Attribute Importance: Identify which attributes matter most to consumers
  • Product Optimisation: Design the product configuration that maximises utility

Full Profile Approach

Respondents evaluate complete product descriptions (all attributes shown simultaneously). More realistic but cognitively demanding. Suited for 4–6 attributes.

Pairwise / Choice-Based

Respondents choose between two complete products (or a product vs. "none"). Choice-Based Conjoint (CBC) is most widely used commercially as it mirrors real purchase decisions.

Part Worth Utility Explained

Each attribute level is assigned a part worth utility. Higher values = stronger preference. Total product utility = sum of all relevant part worths.

Total Utility (product) = ∑ Part Worthattribute level

Part worths are estimated via regression or maximum-likelihood on respondent ratings/rankings/choices.

Advanced Conjoint Methods

Fractional Factorial Design

With many attributes, the full factorial (all combinations) becomes unmanageable:

  • 4 attributes × 3 levels each = 3&sup4; = 81 profiles
  • 6 attributes × 4 levels = 4⁶ = 4,096 profiles

Fractional factorial / orthogonal arrays reduce this to a manageable subset (e.g., 16–32 profiles) while maintaining statistical independence between attribute levels.

Hierarchical Bayesian Conjoint

Estimates individual-level part worths for each respondent (rather than group averages). Benefits:

  • Captures preference heterogeneity within the sample
  • Works with fewer responses per person
  • Enables personalised product recommendations
  • Powers segment discovery

Latent Class Analysis: Groups respondents into segments based on similar preference patterns, enabling targeted product design.

3. Moving Averages in Test Marketing

Why Moving Averages?

Sales data is noisy — it fluctuates day-to-day due to random variation, promotions, and seasonal effects. Moving averages smooth out this noise to reveal the underlying trend, making it easier to evaluate whether a new product is gaining traction.

Three Types of Moving Averages

1Simple Moving Average (SMA)
SMAₙ = (P₁ + P₂ + ... + Pₙ) / n

Average of the most recent n periods. Equal weight to all periods. Best for stable series with no strong trend.

Limitation: Slow to respond to sudden changes; lags the data.

2Weighted Moving Average (WMA)
WMA = 0.5×Pₙ + 0.3×Pₙ₋₁ + 0.2×Pₙ₋₂

More recent periods receive higher weights. Weights must sum to 1. More responsive than SMA while still smoothing noise.

Limitation: Weight selection is arbitrary; weights must be set manually.

3Exponential Moving Average (EMA)
EMAₙ = α×Pₙ + (1−α)×EMAₙ₋₁

α = smoothing factor (0 to 1). Higher α = faster response to recent data. All past observations are included (with exponentially declining weights). Most widely used in practice.

Choosing α for EMA: α=0.9 reacts very quickly to changes (good for detecting trend shifts in test marketing). α=0.1 smooths heavily and is slow to respond (good for long-term trend analysis).

4. Demand Forecasting

Overview & Importance

Demand forecasting estimates future customer demand for a product. For new product decisions, accurate forecasting determines production volumes, supply chain planning, inventory levels, and financial projections. Overforecasting leads to excess inventory; underforecasting results in stockouts and lost sales.

Forecasting Methods by Type

Qualitative Methods

Used when historical data is unavailable (new product launches, disruptive innovations).

  • Delphi Method: Structured anonymous rounds of expert opinion. Experts submit forecasts; facilitator summarises and feeds back; experts revise. Converges toward consensus without group-think.
  • Focus Groups: Small group of target consumers; qualitative insights into demand drivers and barriers to purchase.
  • Sales Force Composite: Bottom-up forecast aggregated from frontline salespeople who know their territories. Risk: salespeople may bias downward to set easier targets.
  • Market Research Surveys: Purchase intention surveys (scale: definitely will buy → definitely will not buy).

Quantitative Methods

Used when historical data exists (established products, line extensions).

  • ARIMA: AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average. Handles trends and seasonality in time series data. Components: AR(p) uses past values; I(d) differencing for stationarity; MA(q) uses past errors.
  • Regression: Y = f(price, advertising, competitor actions, season). Identifies causal drivers of demand.
  • Decomposition: Separates time series into Trend, Seasonal, Cyclical, Irregular components.
  • Exponential Smoothing (Holt-Winters): Extends EMA to handle trend and seasonality simultaneously.

Machine Learning Methods

Decision Trees

Handles nonlinear relationships and interactions between demand drivers. Interpretable rules (e.g., "if price < $15 AND season = summer → high demand").

Random Forest

Ensemble of 100–1,000 decision trees. Reduces overfitting via bagging. Provides feature importance to understand demand drivers.

Neural Networks

Captures complex non-linear patterns. LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) is particularly effective for sequential time series forecasting.

Forecast Accuracy Metrics

MAE = ∑|Actual − Forecast| / n

Mean Absolute Error: Average absolute deviation. In original units. Not sensitive to outliers.

MSE = ∑(Actual − Forecast)² / n

Mean Squared Error: Penalises large errors more heavily. Units are squared (not intuitive).

MAPE = ∑|Actual − Forecast| / Actual × 100% / n

Mean Absolute Percentage Error: Most intuitive — expresses error as % of actual. Easy to communicate to stakeholders.

Forecast Bias = ∑(Forecast − Actual) / n

Positive bias = systematic overforecast. Negative bias = systematic underforecast. Should be close to zero.

Summary & Exam Tips

Key Formulas to Memorise

Market Shareᵢ = Utilityᵢ / ∑ Utilities × 100
Attribute Importance = Rangeᵢ / ∑ Ranges × 100
EMAₙ = α×Pₙ + (1−α)×EMAₙ₋₁
MAPE = ∑|A−F|/A × 100/n
Forecast Bias = ∑(F−A)/n
Total Utility = ∑ Part Worths

Exam Tips

  • Conjoint — Part Worth Utility: Higher part worth = more preferred. Always a relative measure within the study, not an absolute preference. Cannot compare part worths across different conjoint studies.
  • Conjoint — Attribute Importance: ALWAYS calculate as % of total range, not based on highest part worth alone. An attribute with very consistent (small range) preferences is less important even if levels are liked.
  • Market Share Simulation: Shares must sum to 100%. If asked to add a new product, recalculate total utility with the new product included — this will reduce existing products' shares.
  • Moving Averages: SMA lags data and reacts slowly. EMA reacts faster. Higher α = more weight on recent data = faster response. Always smooth deseasonalised data when seasonality is present.
  • MAPE vs. MAE: MAPE is best for reporting to non-technical stakeholders and for comparing across products with different scales. MAE is better when actual values can be near zero.
  • Delphi vs. Sales Force Composite: Delphi uses expert opinion through anonymous rounds. Sales Force Composite uses salespeople bottom-up. Delphi is better for new products; Sales Composite for established products in known territories.
  • Common Exam Trap: Forecast Bias averages out positives and negatives — a bias near zero does NOT mean errors are small (MAE/MAPE can still be high). Use both bias and error metrics together.

Q1 · 2 marks A laptop conjoint study gives these part-worth utilities — Brand: A = +1.2, B = +0.5, C = −0.8; Price: ₹800 = +1.5, ₹1000 = +0.6, ₹1200 = −1.0; Storage: 256 GB = +0.4, 512 GB = +1.1, 1 TB = +1.8; Weight: Light = +0.9, Heavy = −0.3. What is the total utility of the profile Brand B, ₹800, 1 TB, Heavy?
Total utility is the sum of the chosen levels' part-worths.
U = 0.5 (Brand B) + 1.5 (₹800) + 1.8 (1 TB) + (−0.3) (Heavy) = 0.5 + 1.5 + 1.8 − 0.3 = 3.5
5.4 is the best-possible profile (A, ₹800, 1 TB, Light); 4.1 drops the negative Weight term.
Q2 · 2 marks Using the same study, the attribute ranges are Brand = 2.0, Price = 2.5, Storage = 1.4, Weight = 1.2 (sum = 7.1). What is the importance of the Storage attribute (%)?
Importance = that attribute's part-worth range as a share of all ranges.
Storage importance = range / Σranges × 100 = 1.4 / 7.1 × 100 = 19.7%
16.9% is Weight's importance (1.2/7.1); 35.2% is Price's (2.5/7.1).
Q3 · 2 marks Over four weeks the actual demand was 50, 60, 70, 80 units and the forecasts were 48, 63, 66, 84. What is the Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD)?
MAD averages the absolute errors.
|errors| = |50−48|, |60−63|, |70−66|, |80−84| = 2, 3, 4, 4 MAD = (2 + 3 + 4 + 4) / 4 = 13 / 4 = 3.25
13 is the sum (not the mean); 0.25 wrongly keeps signed errors (2−3+4−4 = −1, |·|/4); 4.93 is the MAPE in %.
Q4 · 2 marks An analyst computes a 3-period weighted moving average on demand of 300, 330, 360 (oldest→newest) using weights 1, 2, 3. What forecast results, and how does it compare with the simple moving average (SMA)?
WMA divides the weighted sum by the sum of weights.
WMA = (1×300 + 2×330 + 3×360) / (1+2+3) = (300 + 660 + 1080) / 6 = 2040 / 6 = 340 SMA = (300 + 330 + 360) / 3 = 330
Because the series is rising and the heaviest weight sits on the newest (largest) value, the WMA (340) exceeds the SMA (330). 990 forgets to divide by Σweights.
Q5 · 2 marks A firm runs focus groups and expert Delphi panels to estimate demand for a radically new product that has no historical sales data. How is this forecasting approach best described?
Qualitative (judgemental) forecasting — methods such as the Delphi technique, expert opinion and market research — is used precisely when there is little or no historical data, as for a brand-new product. Quantitative methods (moving averages, causal regression) require a numeric history to extrapolate.
Q6 · 2 marks What is the purpose of estimating part-worth utilities in conjoint analysis?
Part-worths are the utility weights each attribute level adds to overall preference. By decomposing choices this way, conjoint analysis reveals which features customers value most (the attribute with the widest part-worth range is the most important), guiding product design and pricing. Segmentation, time-series forecasting, and error metrics (MAD/MAPE) are separate tasks.
