Week 2 — One-Way ANOVA on Online Ad Channels

Setup

Compare mean conversions across 3 advertising channels. PDF reports the following calculated values:

  • SSB (Between-Group Sum of Squares) = 1616.67
  • dfbetween = 2 (k − 1, with k = 3 groups)
  • MSW (Within-Group Mean Square) = 329.39
  • dfwithin = 12 (so total n = 15)

Step-by-step Solution

1. Mean Square Between (MSB):

MSB = SSB / dfbetween = 1616.67 / 2 = 808.34

2. F-statistic:

F = MSB / MSW = 808.34 / 329.39 ≈ 2.45

3. Decision: Compare F = 2.45 to the critical value of F(2, 12) at α = 0.05. The associated p-value = 0.12 > 0.05, so we fail to reject H0 — there is no statistically significant difference between channel means.

4. Tukey's HSD: Post-hoc test used to identify which specific pairs of means differ — only applied if F is significant.

Week 4 — Conjoint Analysis: Part-Worth Utility (Smartphones)

Attributes & Sample Utility Scores

  • Brand: Apple +1.5, Samsung +0.8, Google −0.3
  • Screen Size: 5.5″ −0.5, 6.1″ (highest), 6.7″ (next)
  • Storage: 64GB, 128GB, 256GB
  • Camera Quality: Standard, Pro, Ultra

Interpret the utilities.

Interpretation

  • Apple has the highest brand utility (+1.5) — most preferred brand among Apple/Samsung/Google.
  • Samsung (+0.8) is the second preference; Google (−0.3) is a lower preference (not a dislike — relative).
  • For screen size, 5.5″ has the lowest preference (−0.5) → larger screens are more desirable.
  • Range comparison: Brand utility range (+1.5 to −0.3) = 1.8; if this is wider than the screen-size range, brand plays a more significant role than screen size in consumer choice.
  • Rule: compare utilities within an attribute, not across attributes.

Week 4 — Moving Averages in Test Marketing

Steps to Calculate a Simple Moving Average (PDF §4.6.1)

  1. Sum the values for the chosen period (e.g., 7 days, 14 days).
  2. Divide by the number of periods to get the average.
  3. As new data arrives, drop the oldest and include the newest — the "moving window".

Types of Moving Averages

  • Simple Moving Average (SMA): equal weight to all points in the window.
  • Weighted Moving Average (WMA): heavier weight on more recent points.
  • Exponential Moving Average (EMA): exponentially decreasing weight for older points.

Worked toy example (7-day SMA of daily sales):

1–710, 12, 14, 13, 15, 16, 149494/7 ≈ 13.43
Day 8 = 18—drop 10, add 18 → 102102/7 ≈ 14.57

Week 8 — Channel Management Numerical Problems

Problem 1 — Sales-Channel ROI

A company invests $150,000 in a new sales channel. The channel generates $250,000 in revenue and incurs $50,000 in operational costs. Find ROI.

Solution

Net Profit = Revenue − (Investment + Operational Cost) = 250,000 − (150,000 + 50,000) = $50,000 ROI = (Net Profit / Investment) × 100 = (50,000 / 150,000) × 100 = 33.3%

Problem 2 — Customer Retention Rate (CRR)

A company starts the year with 1,200 customers, acquires 300 new customers, and ends with 1,100 active customers. Find CRR.

Solution

CRR = (End − New) / Start × 100 = (1,100 − 300) / 1,200 × 100 = 800 / 1,200 × 100 = 66.67%

Problem 3 — Sales Conversion Rate

A channel attracts 5,000 potential customers; 1,250 make a purchase.

Solution

Conversion Rate = (Sales / Potential Customers) × 100 = (1,250 / 5,000) × 100 = 25%

Problem 4 — Market Penetration Rate

A new product reaches 40,000 customers out of 200,000 potential customers.

Solution

Market Penetration = (Reached / Potential) × 100 = (40,000 / 200,000) × 100 = 20%

Week 8 — Advertising Effectiveness Numerical Problems

Problem 1 — Click-Through Rate (CTR)

Impressions = 50,000; Clicks = 2,000.

Solution

CTR = (Clicks / Impressions) × 100 = (2,000 / 50,000) × 100 = 4%

Problem 2 — Cost Per Acquisition (CPA)

Total Ad Spend = $10,000; Conversions = 500 customers.

Solution

CPA = Ad Spend / Conversions = 10,000 / 500 = $20 per customer

Problem 3 — Return on Investment (ROI)

Revenue = $25,000; Ad Spend = $10,000.

Solution

ROI = (Revenue − Cost) / Cost × 100 = (25,000 − 10,000) / 10,000 × 100 = 150%

Problem 4 — Conversion Rate

Total Visitors = 5,000; Conversions = 250.

Solution

Conversion Rate = (250 / 5,000) × 100 = 5%

Problem 5 — Cost Per Click (CPC)

Total Ad Spend = $3,000; Clicks = 6,000.

Solution

CPC = Ad Spend / Clicks = 3,000 / 6,000 = $0.50 per click

Week 9 — Return on Marketing Investment (ROMI)

Problem

A marketing campaign generates $500,000 in revenue. Compute ROMI given typical formula.

ROMI Formula

ROMI = (Incremental Revenue from Marketing − Marketing Cost) / Marketing Cost × 100

A positive ROMI means the campaign generated more value than its cost; a higher ROMI = more efficient marketing spend.

Week 12 — Z-Score Outlier Detection

Method

The Z-score measures how far each data point lies from the mean in standard-deviation units.

Z = (x − μ) / σ

Steps

  1. Compute mean μ and standard deviation σ of the dataset.
  2. For each point, compute Z.
  3. Apply threshold:
    • |Z| > 2 → moderate outlier
    • |Z| > 3 → extreme outlier
  4. Flag any point exceeding the threshold.

Python Snippet (from PDF)

threshold = 3 mean_y = np.mean(variable) std_y = np.std(variable) z_scores = np.abs((variable − mean_y) / std_y) outliers_z = variable[z_scores > threshold]

Week 12 — IQR Outlier Detection

Method

IQR = Q3 − Q1 Lower Bound = Q1 − 1.5 × IQR Upper Bound = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR

Data points outside [Lower, Upper] are outliers.

Worked toy example

Suppose Q1 = 20, Q3 = 60 → IQR = 40.

  • Lower Bound = 20 − 1.5 × 40 = 20 − 60 = −40
  • Upper Bound = 60 + 1.5 × 40 = 60 + 60 = 120
  • A data point of 130 → 130 > 120 → outlier.
  • A data point of 0 → within [−40, 120] → not an outlier.

Python Snippet (from PDF)

Q1 = np.percentile(variable, 25) Q3 = np.percentile(variable, 75) IQR = Q3 - Q1 lower_bound = Q1 - 1.5 * IQR upper_bound = Q3 + 1.5 * IQR outliers = variable[(variable < lower_bound) | (variable > upper_bound)]

Box plots visualise this directly — the box is the IQR, whiskers extend 1.5×IQR, and points outside are outliers.
